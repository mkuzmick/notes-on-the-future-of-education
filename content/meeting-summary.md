## Executive Summary: Oral Assessments in the Age of AI

### 1. The Challenge

As AI use in coursework approaches ubiquity — 88% of students at McGill reported using generative AI in Fall 2025, and detection tools remain unreliable — the unaccompanied written essay can no longer reliably demonstrate what a student actually understands. Faculty across Harvard and peer institutions are exploring oral formats as one part of a broader reassessment of how we verify student learning. 

However, "oral assessment" is not one thing: it ranges from five-minute homework check-ins to hour-long comprehensive exams, and the design choices involved (scale, stakes, scaffolding, grading) matter.

### 2. Headline Advice

The research and faculty experience converge on a practical finding: low-stakes, frequent oral work (brief check-ins, reading explanations, oral defenses of written work) consistently improves engagement and learning outcomes, while high-stakes one-shot oral exams can produce equity concerns and student backlash. 

The value of oral assessment is not primarily about preventing AI cheating, but a dialogue that reveals understanding in ways written artifacts cannot. But every oral format has costs (time, equity risks, reliability challenges), and the right approach depends on class size, discipline, and what you're trying to assess.

Key points for a faculty audience:
- **Start with low stakes and high frequency.** Weekly five-minute "argument audits" or oral defenses of written work are more effective — and more equitable — than replacing a final paper with a single oral exam.
- **Oral assessment is not new at Harvard.** Different schools and departments have practiced it continuously for decades. 
- **AI resistance is a bonus, not the primary rationale.** Oral formats cultivate synthesis, real-time reasoning, and professional readiness — skills that matter regardless of AI.
- **Every format has trade-offs.** Written exams test what AI does best (linear analytic prose); oral exams test what AI can't do (spontaneous, situated dialogue) — but they introduce new equity and reliability challenges that must be actively managed.

### 3. A Taxonomy of Approaches — with Easy First Steps

The oral assessment formats discussed in our meeting and in the research fall into several distinct categories. Faculty don't need to choose one, as many of these can be combined or sequenced. The first three are relatively low-commitment entry points; the rest require more course redesign.

#### Oral Defense of Written Work
*Add a conversation to an existing paper assignment.*

Students submit a written assignment, then sit for a 10–15 minute conversation with a TF about their argument, sources, and reasoning. This is the Cornell model: "be prepared to orally discuss any content included in your paper." It doesn't require redesigning the course, deters pure AI generation, and builds oral fluency. The mere expectation of oral defense changes how students approach the writing.

#### Recurring Oral Check-Ins
*Brief, low-stakes, high-frequency.*

Five-minute "argument audits" where a student explains one reading or concept aloud to their TF — weekly or biweekly. At Michigan, adding weekly check-ins increased engagement and improved final grades. The format matters less than the frequency and stakes: students who practice speaking about course material regularly arrive at higher-stakes moments better prepared and less anxious.

#### The Rubric and the Practice Run
*The single most effective intervention.*

Structured practice — specifically mock oral exams — raised first-time pass rates from 76% to 100% in one study, and 77%+ of students found them "very or extremely helpful." Share a sample rubric, do a practice round in section, and make the criteria transparent. At History & Literature, preparation includes a mock oral exam conducted by two faculty members not on the real exam panel. Practice grading sessions for TFs are equally important: grading calibration is essential and often overlooked.

#### The Crit
*Presentation and defense before peers and faculty.*

The art critique — where a student presents work and defends choices before a group of peers and instructors — is an established oral assessment format in studio disciplines. It's inherently dialogic: the student must respond to questions, pushback, and reinterpretation in real time. Faculty in our meeting raised the crit as a model worth adapting beyond studio contexts — any course where students produce a project, analysis, or interpretation could use a crit-style defense. Lauren Kaminsky's framing of "the freedom to riff" captures the spirit: creating space for genuine intellectual exchange rather than rote performance.

#### Scaffolded Capstone Oral Exam
*Structure the whole course around it.*

Following the History & Literature model, low-stakes oral presentations in tutorials build toward a high-stakes senior oral exam (one hour, three examiners, five prepared topics — running continuously since 1906). This requires scaffolding throughout the semester: naming learning objectives early, building in pauses for practice, and giving students explicit preparation. Faculty in our meeting noted the key design question: "Where would I build in pauses? How much do I tell them in advance?" Social Studies takes a similar approach: a 1.5-hour comprehensive oral, mandatory since 1960, preceded by a 1,000-word intellectual autobiography.

#### Performance-Based Assessment
*In disciplines where doing is part of what is studied.*

Folklore & Mythology requires all concentrators to complete a general examination with oral components — a format philosophically aligned with a concentration studying how human societies preserve knowledge through spoken word. Faculty noted we should look at disciplines where performance is already part of what is studied (music, theater, folklore) for transferable models. The principle: if your discipline values a kind of doing, the assessment should involve that doing.

#### AI-Augmented Oral Exam (Bok Center / Learning Lab Pilot)
*Scalable, data-rich, multimodal.*

Sarah Craycraft piloted an AI-augmented oral exam in a Gen Ed course on folklore and performance, supported by the Learning Lab. Students drew cards from thematic decks (Core Concepts, Case Studies, Theoretical Models), prepared for five minutes, then delivered a 4–6 minute spontaneous presentation. The system generated follow-up questions from each student's transcript, and all session data were stored for grading calibration — addressing the documentation gap that makes traditional oral exams hard to norm.

Results (n=75, >90% response rate): 86% said preparation deepened understanding, but only 55% would choose the format again — resistance centered on recording and the studio environment, not the oral assessment itself. The card-deck prompt system works as a standalone technique even without the full tech setup.

#### Board Talk
*Real-time problem-solving at the whiteboard — standard in STEM.*

In physics, math, and engineering, students solve problems at a whiteboard while explaining their reasoning aloud to an instructor or peers. The format makes thinking visible in real time: an examiner can see not just whether a student gets the right answer but how they approach the problem, where they get stuck, and whether they can recover. Board talks are common in graduate qualifying exams and increasingly used in undergraduate physics and astronomy courses, where questions structured around explanation, application, and extension (following Bloom's taxonomy) reveal reasoning that written problem sets miss. The format scales naturally in smaller courses and lab sections, and the artifact is the board itself — photographable, reviewable, and harder to fake than a written solution.

#### Generals and Graduate Oral Exams
*Established models at the highest stakes.*

Graduate programs across disciplines have never stopped using oral assessment. The PhD qualifying exam ("generals") — typically a multi-hour oral defense before a faculty committee — remains the standard gatekeeping mechanism in most fields. In the sciences, it often involves a board talk component. In the humanities and social sciences, it tests breadth across fields and the ability to synthesize under questioning. History & Literature's undergraduate senior oral is modeled on this tradition: five prepared topics, three examiners, one hour. Social Studies' 1.5-hour comprehensive oral has run since 1960. These formats demonstrate that oral assessment is not experimental — it is the proven standard for verifying deep understanding at the highest level of the university. The design question for undergraduates is how to adapt the rigor and structure of generals for courses where students have less preparation time and lower stakes.

#### Skill-Based Presentation
*Students teach what they've learned to learn.*

Sarah Craycraft has also used an approach where students choose a skill they must figure out how to learn, then present on both the skill and their learning process. This foregrounds metacognition — the assessment is not just "do you know this?" but "can you reflect on how you came to know it?" — and gives students agency over the content of their oral performance.

- **Build a practical "forking tree" guide for faculty.** Based on your class size, course type, comfort with technology, and assessment goals — here are the oral formats most likely to work for you, with testimonials, examples, and rubrics for each path. The taxonomy above is a starting point; the guide would make it actionable.

### 5. What the Evidence Shows — Successes and Failures

**What works:**
- **Low stakes, high frequency.** The pattern across post-AI classroom experiments is consistent: brief recurring oral work improves outcomes; high-stakes one-shot substitutions backfire. (Inside Higher Ed, 2024; EDUCAUSE, 2024)
- **Scaffolding and practice.** Mock exams, rubrics, and explicit criteria reduce anxiety and improve performance dramatically. History & Literature's rehearsal model is a proof of concept.
- **Grading can be easier, not harder.** Catherine Hartmann (University of Wyoming) found that oral exams for a 25-student humanities course took 13 hours total — less than the 15+ hours for written midterms and finals. Lauren Kaminsky noted reflecting on student performance at the end of each exam session, using the last 10 minutes for structured self-assessment.
- **Scaffolded oral assessment nearly eliminates academic dishonesty.** At Queensborough Community College (CUNY), 74% mastery, 90% proficiency, dishonesty "almost irrelevant." (Akkaraju, 2023)
- **Oral components can be *more* accessible.** A California community college that shifted finals in-person found optional oral components were more accessible for students with test anxiety and learning disabilities — challenging the assumption that oral formats are inherently exclusionary.

**What to watch out for:**
- **High-stakes substitution without scaffolding.** UC Davis replaced a twenty-page paper with a twenty-minute oral defense; student evaluations dropped by 0.8 points, with complaints centered on anxiety and fairness.
- **Gender bias.** Women received systematically lower oral exam scores than men across ten UK universities, with the gap widening under male examiners. (Studies in Higher Education, 2018)
- **Inter-rater reliability.** Oral exams (correlation 0.4–0.6) are substantially less reliable than written work (0.7–0.85). Multiple examiners and rubrics help but add cost.
- **Student resistance to technology, not to oral assessment.** Sarah Craycraft's pilot showed that students valued the oral format but objected to elements needed for scaling — recording, AI follow-ups, and the studio setting. The tension between documentation (needed for fairness) and the human feel of conversation (needed for authenticity) doesn't resolve; it has to be managed.

### 6. Unanswered Questions

- **What terminology should we use?** "Oral assessments," "oral assignments," "oral activities," "oral exams" — each carries different connotations about stakes and formality. The group noted this matters for framing and faculty buy-in.
- **How do you scale?** The logistics for a 150-student lecture are fundamentally different from a 12-person seminar. The TA bottleneck is real: 150 students x 15-minute exams = 37.5 hours of exam time, plus scheduling, plus training.
- **How do you handle recording and transcripts?** Recording addresses fairness and documentation but raises anxiety, FERPA, and privacy concerns. Sarah Craycraft's pilot showed students valued the format but resisted being recorded. Lauren Kaminsky raised questions about using transcripts and technology. Can we find the right balance?
- **How can AI tools support preparation without undermining assessment?** Generating key terms, offering practice questions, producing transcripts for grading calibration — AI could help on the scaffolding side. But the line between preparation support and assessment compromise needs careful thought.
- **What about accommodations?** Standard written-exam accommodations don't transfer straightforwardly. We need guidance from Accessibility.
- **Can we plan summer workshops?** A faculty teach-in where instructors try oral assessment formats, practice grading, and share case studies — format, timing, and scope are open.
- **What does the "forking tree" look like?** A decision-tree resource: how big is your class? What type of course? How much tech do you want to use? What are you trying to assess? — with testimonials, examples, and rubrics for each path.
- **What about discussion as assessment?** The meeting raised establishing rules of discussion, the difference between written and oral assignments, and whether group discussion can be graded — these deserve more investigation.
- **Where are the sample rubrics?** Faculty want concrete examples. We need rubrics for each format in the taxonomy above — oral defense, check-in, crit, capstone, AI-augmented, performance-based.
