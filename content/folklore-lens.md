# The Folklore Lens

Oral assessment through the study of oral traditions.

## Why Folklore

Folklore and oral tradition studies offer a distinctive angle on the oral assessment conversation — not because they advocate for oral exams, but because they have spent a century studying what oral performance actually is, how oral knowledge differs from literate knowledge, and what happens when cultures shift between the two. The disciplines that study speech as a primary mode of knowing have relevant findings for anyone designing assessment around it.

Harvard's own [Folklore & Mythology](https://folkmyth.fas.harvard.edu/concentration-requirements) concentration (founded 1967) requires all concentrators — not just honors students — to complete a general examination with both written and oral components in their final term. A concentration studying how human societies preserved knowledge through spoken word requires oral examination. The alignment between subject matter and assessment format is not accidental.

## Walter Ong: How Oral Cultures Think

The foundational text is Walter Ong's *Orality and Literacy: The Technologizing of the Word* (1982), which demonstrated that oral and literate cultures don't just communicate differently — they *think* differently.[^1]

Ong identified nine characteristics of oral thought that distinguish it from literate cognition:

1. **Additive** rather than subordinate — stringing ideas with "and" rather than building nested hierarchies
2. **Aggregative** rather than analytic — preferring clusters ("brave soldier," "sturdy oak") over parsed, disaggregated elements
3. **Redundant** — repeating and restating for memory, since there is no text to refer back to
4. **Conservative** — "wise elders" preserve knowledge; innovation risks losing the archive
5. **Close to the human lifeworld** — situational and concrete rather than abstract
6. **Agonistic** — knowledge embedded in struggle, debate, and verbal contest
7. **Empathetic and participatory** — "getting with it" rather than standing back from it
8. **Homeostatic** — sloughing off irrelevant memories; knowledge is what the community currently needs
9. **Situational** rather than abstract — thinking in terms of operations and contexts, not categories and definitions

The implications for assessment are significant. Written exams test the ability to produce what Ong calls "sparsely linear or analytic thought" — an "artificial creation" compared to oral redundancy's naturalness. Writing "imposes strain on the psyche" by forcing decontextualized, abstract reasoning. Oral assessment, by contrast, tests the ability to engage in additive, aggregative, responsive, situated thinking — knowledge performed in context rather than extracted from it.

This doesn't make one mode superior. It means they test *different cognitive capacities*. When we default to written assessment exclusively, we privilege a narrow cognitive mode and treat it as the whole of understanding.

## The Oral Formula: Composition in Performance

One of the major discoveries of twentieth-century folklore scholarship was Milman Parry and Albert Lord's demonstration that Homeric epic was not written and then recited, but *composed in the act of performance*.[^2] The singer of tales did not memorize a fixed text. Each performance was a new composition, built from traditional formulas, themes, and story patterns that the singer had internalized through years of apprenticeship.

This finding reframed what "knowing" means in an oral context. The Homeric bard didn't "know" the Iliad the way a student knows a textbook — as a fixed body of information to be retrieved. The bard knew *how to perform* the tradition — how to assemble elements in real time, responsive to audience, occasion, and the flow of the narrative itself. Knowledge was generative, not reproductive.

The parallel to oral examination is direct. A student in a written exam retrieves and arranges information. A student in an oral exam must *generate* responses in real time — assembling knowledge from internalized frameworks, responsive to the examiner's questions, the direction of the conversation, and the logic of the argument as it unfolds. The cognitive demands are closer to the oral poet's composition-in-performance than to the literate scholar's essay.

## Brian Street: The Ideology of Literacy

Brian Street's New Literacy Studies added a critical dimension to the oral-literate framework.[^3] Street distinguished between two models of literacy:

**The autonomous model** treats writing as a neutral, context-independent skill — a technology that uniformly enhances cognition regardless of social setting. Under this model, written assessment is naturally "more objective" than oral.

**The ideological model** recognizes that what counts as "good writing" is shaped by power structures, disciplinary conventions, and social context. Writing is not a neutral test of cognition. It is a *practice* embedded in specific institutional and cultural settings.

If we accept Street's analysis, the assumption that written assessment is inherently more objective than oral is itself an ideological position — a product of institutional inertia rather than empirical evidence. Written and oral assessment are equally context-dependent. The question of which to use is pedagogical and political, not epistemological.

This matters for the current conversation because much of the resistance to oral assessment rests on the assumption that written work provides a stable, neutral baseline. Street's framework suggests that baseline was never neutral — it was the product of a specific literacy ideology that valorized decontextualized, individual, text-based performance.

## Derrida and the Complication

Jacques Derrida's critique of "phonocentrism" complicates any simple romanticization of speech over writing.[^4] Derrida argued that the Western philosophical tradition has privileged speech as more "authentic," more "present," more "real" than writing — a hierarchy he called the "metaphysics of presence." Speech seems immediate; writing seems secondary, derivative, a mere record of the living voice.

Derrida dismantled this hierarchy. Speech, he argued, is not inherently closer to truth than writing. Both are representations. Both are mediated. Both are subject to misinterpretation and ambiguity. The dream of "pure" communication — unmediated access to the speaker's meaning — is an illusion regardless of the medium.

This is a useful corrective in the oral assessment context. Oral examination is not a return to some pristine state of authentic communication. A student performing in front of an examiner is performing — managing impressions, selecting which version of their understanding to display, navigating the social dynamics of the interaction. The "liveness" of oral assessment does not automatically equal transparency or authenticity. It is a *different mode of assessment* with different strengths and different vulnerabilities.

## Cicero's Five Canons: What Written Assessment Lost

Cicero's *De Oratore* (55 BCE) identified five canons of rhetoric — the five capacities required for effective communication:[^5]

1. **Invention** — generating arguments and ideas
2. **Arrangement** — organizing ideas into effective structure
3. **Style** — expressing clearly, persuasively, and appropriately
4. **Memory** — internalizing knowledge for retrieval without external aids
5. **Delivery** — embodying argument physically: voice, gesture, presence

Modern written assessment tests at most three of these: Invention, Arrangement, and Style. It has abandoned Memory (open-book exams and research papers allow external retrieval) and Delivery (no physical performance required).

Oral assessment engages all five. A student defending a thesis orally must generate arguments (Invention), organize them coherently under pressure (Arrangement), express them clearly (Style), retrieve relevant knowledge without notes (Memory), and embody the argument with voice, pace, and presence (Delivery).

From a folklore perspective, the abandonment of Memory and Delivery represents a significant narrowing of what we assess. Oral tradition cultures understood that knowledge *held in the body* — memorized, rehearsed, performed — is qualitatively different from knowledge accessed through external aids. The student who can explain a concept from memory has a different relationship to that knowledge than the student who can look it up. Whether that difference matters for a given learning outcome is a design question, not a settled one.

## Performance Theory: What Folklorists Know About "Performing" Knowledge

Folklore scholarship has developed a rich vocabulary for analyzing oral performance that is relevant to assessment design.[^6] Key concepts include:

**Competence vs. performance**: A speaker's underlying knowledge (competence) is distinct from what they display in any given situation (performance). Performance is always partial, situational, shaped by audience, context, and the performer's state. This maps onto a central concern in oral assessment: the gap between what a student knows and what they manage to demonstrate under exam conditions.

**Emergent quality**: Oral performance is "emergent" — it takes shape in the interaction between performer and audience. The meaning is not pre-scripted; it arises from the exchange. This is exactly what distinguishes oral from written assessment: the examiner's questions shape the student's responses, which shape the next questions, producing an exchange that neither party could have predicted. The assessment itself is a co-production.

**Keying**: Performers signal that "this is a performance" through specific cues — tone, register, formality, framing devices. Students in oral exams similarly shift into a performance register. Folklorists observe that being *aware* of this shift — understanding that you are performing knowledge, not just possessing it — is itself a competency. Whether assessment should test that meta-awareness is another design question.

**Audience co-authorship**: In oral traditions, the audience is never passive. They respond, they challenge, they shape the direction of the performance. The examiner in an oral exam functions as this kind of active audience — not a neutral receiver but a participant whose reactions, follow-up questions, and expressions of interest or skepticism shape what the student says next. This is fundamentally different from the relationship between a student and a written prompt.

## The Paradox of Documentation

Folklorists have long grappled with a problem that maps directly onto oral assessment design: the moment you record an oral performance, you change its nature. A folk tale transcribed is no longer a folk tale — it's a text. An oral exam recorded for review is no longer purely oral — it becomes a document, subject to the dynamics of textual analysis rather than the dynamics of live exchange.

This is not merely a philosophical point. UK universities that mandate recording all summative oral assessments have found that students behave differently when recorded. The "liveness" that oral assessment is supposed to test — spontaneity, responsiveness, the ability to think in the moment — is altered by the awareness of documentation. The recording paradox is a version of the observer effect: measurement changes the thing being measured.

Folklorists have no solution to this problem. They document it, theorize it, and design their fieldwork around it. The same honest acknowledgment is needed in oral assessment design: recording is necessary for fairness and accountability, and it changes the nature of the interaction. Both things are true.

## Oral Tradition and Equity

Folklore scholarship also complicates the equity conversation around oral assessment. The discipline has documented extensive evidence that oral traditions are not confined to elite rhetorical cultures. They are universal human practices — present in every culture, every language, every social class. The assumption that oral performance is the province of the privileged (debaters, prep school students, the verbally confident) reflects a specific Western, literate bias.

At the same time, folklorists are clear-eyed about power dynamics in oral contexts. Who gets to speak? Whose speech is valued? Whose style of argument is recognized as "articulate"? These questions are central to folklore scholarship, and they map directly onto the bias concerns in oral assessment literature. The solution folklore suggests is not avoidance but *attention to the terms of performance* — who sets the rules, what counts as competent performance, and whether those rules encode assumptions about whose speech sounds "right."

## What This Lens Offers

The folklore and oral tradition lens does not resolve the question of whether to expand oral assessment. It reframes what the question involves:

**Oral and written are not just different formats — they are different cognitive modes.** Ong's framework suggests that oral assessment tests capacities (situational thinking, responsiveness, participatory knowledge) that written assessment structurally cannot reach. Whether those capacities matter for a given course is a curricular decision.

**Performance is always partial.** What a student demonstrates orally is shaped by context, anxiety, audience, and the specific questions asked. This is true of written assessment too — but the dynamics are different, and oral assessment makes them more visible.

**Documentation changes the thing documented.** Recording oral exams is necessary and distorting. Designing for this tension rather than wishing it away is the responsible path.

**Orality is not a primitive stage to be surpassed.** The deep history of human knowledge transmission is oral. Written assessment is the recent innovation. Neither is inherently superior. The question is fit for purpose.
