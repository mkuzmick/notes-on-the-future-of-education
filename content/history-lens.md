# The Historical Lens

Oral assessment through the long arc of educational history.

## The Medieval University Was an Oral Institution

The modern university was born oral. When the University of Bologna was founded in 1088 and the University of Paris emerged around 1150, the entire curriculum was organized around three oral practices: *Lectio* (reading aloud and commenting on authoritative texts), *Disputatio* (structured oral debate on philosophical and theological questions), and *Quaestio* (resolving contradictions between authorities through oral argument). For the next five centuries, oral disputation was not supplementary to the assessment of learning — it was the assessment itself.

In medieval Oxford and Cambridge, all examinations were "public, oral and in Latin." The format was not a monologue but a multi-party event: challenge and defense, often in a sequence involving several challengers. It was "public negotiation not just between examiner and examinee, but also between several participants, since students were disputing with one another and with any graduates who might choose to intervene."[^1] Assessment was a community practice, not an individual performance for a single evaluator.

Medieval educators understood something that contemporary cognitive science has rediscovered: dialogue reveals understanding in ways monologue cannot. When a student must defend a thesis against a skilled interlocutor — responding to objections, clarifying ambiguities, synthesizing across domains in real time — they demonstrate a quality of understanding that a written document, however polished, cannot fully capture.

## Harvard's Founding: 1636–1643

When Harvard was founded in 1636, the entire theory of education was oral. The earliest published account of the College, *New England's First Fruits* (1643), describes the assessment model in detail: "their publick declamations in Latine and Greeke, and Disputations Logicall and Philosophicall" occurred monthly "in the audience of the Magistrates, Ministers, and other Schollars, for the probation of their growth in Learning."[^2]

Admission itself required oral demonstration: students had to read classical Latin authors extemporaneously and compose "true Latin" in verse and prose — *suo ut aiunt Marte* (by their own hand, as they say). The curriculum was structured around oral performance at every stage:

- **Second year**: Logic and Physics, examined orally
- **Third year**: Ethics and Politics
- **Greek**: Etymology, Syntax, Prosodia, and Dialects
- **Hebrew and Eastern Languages**: Grammar, Chaldee, Syriac
- **Rhetoric**: Declamations by every scholar, monthly

The document also records the actual theses students defended — spanning grammar ("Knowledge of languages is most useful"), logic ("Universals are not outside the intellect"), ethics ("Prudence is the most difficult virtue"), physics ("Whatever moves is moved by another"), and metaphysics ("Every being is good"). These were not written examinations. They were oral performances before the community, subject to challenge.

The main stated purpose of this education: "to advance Learning, and perpetuate it to Posterity; dreading to leave an illiterate Ministry to the Churches, when our present Ministers shall lie in the Dust." Assessment was public, oral, and oriented toward ensuring the transmission of understanding from one generation to the next.

## The Shift to Written Exams: Cambridge and Oxford, 1700–1900

The shift from oral to written examinations was neither inevitable nor universal. Christopher Stray's study of Cambridge and Oxford between 1700 and 1900 documents how specific institutional, intellectual, and political conditions drove the transition — and how the two universities diverged.[^3]

**The first written exam** is attributed to Richard Bentley at Trinity College, Cambridge (1702), who was "of opinion that this oral test was not satisfactory in an enquiry so extensive and profound" and ordered written exercises instead. Bentley's likely motives included improving fellowship quality and controlling outcomes — not a pedagogical discovery about the superiority of writing.

**The Newtonian connection** is critical. The emergence of mathematical ranking at Cambridge was tied to Newtonian natural philosophy's dominance. Mathematics demanded precise numerical differentiation between candidates. Richard Laughton, proctor in 1710–1711, promoted both Newtonian theses and the ranking system. The written exam and numerical grading developed together because the subject matter demanded them — not because oral assessment had been shown to be inadequate.

**Cambridge vs. Oxford**: The two universities followed strikingly different paths:

- Cambridge, dominated by mathematics, adopted strict numerical ranking and completed the shift to printed written papers by 1839. "Subject matter and assessment by precise rank order had an elective affinity."
- Oxford, dominated by classics and humanities, retained oral traditions much longer. It used Greek letter marking (alpha, beta, gamma) rather than numbers, and held regular vivas into the 1920s and 1930s. "The rhetoric of display was part of the culture of gentlemanly orality."

Cambridge used numbers; Oxford used letters. Cambridge ranked individuals; Oxford assessed community membership. The divergence illustrates that the choice between oral and written was always disciplinary and political — shaped by what each institution valued, not by a neutral evaluation of which format tested understanding better.

**Rising student numbers** delivered the final blow. By 1911, Oxford examiners were conducting "80 vivas a day and the system was acknowledged to be a 'blasphemous farce.'" Scale made individual oral assessment logistically impossible in the forms then practiced.

**The philosophical shift** underlying the transition was from socio-moral assessment of groups (who you are, what community you belong to) to cognitive assessment of individuals (what you know, ranked against peers). The removal of "honorary optimes" — inserted into rankings for social status rather than academic merit — reflects this move. Written exams encoded a theory of assessment as individual cognitive measurement. Oral exams had encoded a different theory: assessment as community performance.

## The American Story: Progressive Education and the Loss of Orality

In the United States, the Progressive Education movement of the early twentieth century delivered the final blow to routine oral assessment. John Dewey's emphasis on experiential learning, while philosophically rich, was implemented in ways that devalued formal oral performance and memorization. A [1953 *Atlantic* essay](https://www.theatlantic.com/magazine/archive/1953/04/who-wants-progressive-education-the-influence-of-john-dewey-on-the-public-schools/640458/) by Albert Lynd captures the cultural moment: traditional education's emphasis on oral recitation was swept aside in favor of "growth and richness and joy."

The irony is that Dewey's own pragmatism — his insistence that "knowledge is begotten and exercised in action" — supports oral assessment, which tests knowledge-in-action rather than knowledge-as-artifact. But Progressive Education's implementation narrowed the pedagogical repertoire, and oral examination was lost from mainstream American undergraduate education.

Meanwhile, at Harvard itself, the 1922 Burbank report on the tutorial system in History, Government, and Economics documents faculty grappling with the same scalability concerns that had undone Oxford's vivas. The tutorial system was designed for intensive, small-group oral pedagogy, but even then faculty worried about time costs for "numerous" candidates. The hybrid compromise — written exams supplemented by oral tutorials — was the institutional response. That compromise held for decades, and the oral component gradually became optional, then exceptional, then forgotten in most departments.

## What Never Stopped

The American abandonment of undergraduate oral assessment was geographically and historically specific. Other systems maintained or expanded oral examination:

**France**: The [Classes Préparatoires](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classe_pr%C3%A9paratoire_aux_grandes_%C3%A9coles) conduct twice-weekly oral examinations (*colles*) across all subjects for two years — roughly 200+ oral sessions per student before university entrance. This is the longest continuous tradition of systematic oral assessment in the Western world.

**Germany**: Universities maintained oral STEM examinations throughout the twentieth century. The *mündliche Prüfung* (oral examination) remains standard across disciplines.

**India**: The civil service examination — the gateway to the nation's most prestigious administrative positions — culminates in a mandatory 275-mark viva voce.

**Italy**: Universities conducted five oral exams per day during COVID lockdowns, adapting the format rather than abandoning it.

**The PhD defense**: The *viva voce* for doctoral degrees was never abandoned anywhere in the world. The format that tests the deepest level of academic expertise has always been oral.

## John Quincy Adams and the Boylston Chair

One thread in the historical literature connects oral assessment to democratic practice. Jay Heinrichs documented how John Quincy Adams held the Boylston Chair of Rhetoric at Harvard in 1806 and taught oral persuasion as "the paramount democratic skill." Adams had studied with Benjamin Franklin and heard Edmund Burke. He understood rhetoric not as ornament but as civic infrastructure — the ability to coax reasonable people into public decisions through spoken argument.

After Adams, the Boylston Chair "drifted away from oral address, political meaning, and anything remotely practical." Harvard fell "in thrall to" Petrus Ramus, the sixteenth-century logician who compartmentalized the liberal arts and stripped rhetoric of its persuasive, oral power. Rhetoric became "style, a corpse in brilliant clothing."[^4]

Whether or not one accepts Heinrichs's normative framing, the historical fact is clear: Harvard once located oral argument at the center of its educational mission, and that position eroded over the nineteenth and twentieth centuries.

## The 2020s: AI and the Return of the Question

The current conversation about oral assessment occurs against this long historical arc. The literature suggests several framings:

**The cyclical view**: Each communication technology — writing, print, computers, AI — triggers anxiety about authentic understanding and a turn toward dialogue as corrective. The current moment is one more iteration of a pattern that begins with Plato's *Phaedrus*.

**The structural view**: Written exams dominated because of scale demands (industrialization, rising enrollment) and disciplinary culture (Newtonian mathematics' preference for ranking). AI has disrupted the scale argument (technology can now assist with logistics) and exposed the cultural assumption (that written output equals understanding).

**The contingency view**: The American abandonment of oral assessment was not an advance in assessment science. It was a historical accident driven by logistics, ideology, and institutional imitation. Other systems made different choices with different results.

**The cautionary view**: The shift from oral to written exams was itself a democratizing move — broadening access beyond those who could perform well in the social dynamics of face-to-face examination. Any return to oral formats must reckon with the equity concerns that motivated the original shift.

The historical evidence does not resolve the question of what to do now. It reframes it: the current state of assessment is not a neutral baseline. It is the product of specific institutional choices, made for specific reasons, under specific conditions — many of which no longer obtain.
