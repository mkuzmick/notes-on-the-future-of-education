# Teaching Writing After AI

What writing education becomes when intelligence is collaborative.

## Writing Is No Longer Purely Human-Authored

The premise has already shifted: students are not the sole producers of text anymore. That's not a future problem—it's the current condition. So the question isn't "how do we prevent AI use?" but what writing becomes when intelligence is collaborative.

We can keep designing assignments that assume a single author working alone, or we can ask what it actually means to write well when powerful systems are available to everyone. The second question is harder, but it's the only one that matters now.

## AI Literacy Means Learning How to Learn with Systems

The phrase "AI literacy" gets thrown around loosely, but it shouldn't mean prompting tips or tool familiarity. At its core, AI literacy is learning how to learn with systems—directing them, evaluating their outputs, iterating and refining, and interrogating the model itself. That last part matters: students need to learn to push against a system's own answers, to resist its sycophantic tendencies, to treat fluency as a warning sign rather than a guarantee.

This is a genuine intellectual skill, not a technical one. It requires the same critical disposition we've always wanted students to develop—just applied to a new kind of interlocutor.

## From Producing Text to Shaping Thinking

LLMs don't hold positions. They follow trajectories implied by prompts. This means the writer's role shifts fundamentally: from producing text to setting intellectual direction, running counter-arguments, and designing inquiry loops. Good writing becomes structured thinking across multiple passes, often mediated by AI.

The old model assumed that the hard part was getting words on the page. The new model recognizes that the hard part is knowing what's worth saying—and being rigorous enough to test whether you've actually said it. Writing becomes a process of orchestration and judgment, not just production.

## Advanced Writing as Multi-Step, Multi-Agent Workflow

Beyond basic usage lies something much more ambitious: breaking complex intellectual work into subtasks and managing them across multiple passes. Research, synthesis, critique, style transformation—each can be handled as a distinct stage, with the writer functioning as architect and editor of the whole system.

This starts to look less like essay writing and more like research infrastructure. Students working at this level manage outputs in structured formats—JSON, databases, notebooks—and design workflows that are reproducible and auditable. The essay becomes one possible output of a much larger process.

## Context Is Everything

The quality of AI-mediated writing depends entirely on what you feed the system. This makes context engineering—curating sources, preparing clean materials, anchoring outputs to real documents—a core writing skill. Students need to learn how to build the knowledge base that grounds their work.

This is where RAG, retrieval, and careful source preparation become part of the writing curriculum. Not as technical add-ons, but as fundamental practices. Writing becomes partly a data preparation and context engineering problem, and the students who understand this will produce dramatically better work.

## Beyond Consumer AI to Developer-Level Literacy

There's a strong distinction between how students currently use AI—chat interface, one-shot prompting—and what we should be teaching. The goal is developer-level literacy: APIs, structured outputs, pipelines, reproducible workflows. This is analogous to moving from using Word to building a publishing system.

Not every student needs to write code. But every student should understand that there's a layer beneath the chat interface where real power lives—and that learning to work at that layer is what separates shallow use from genuine intellectual leverage.

## Writing as Multimodal, Performative, and Interactive

Writing is no longer just text. It becomes video, performance, interactive systems, installations. AI enables real-time transformation and augmentation—live footnotes, translations, expansions, parallel versions. The essay becomes just one format among many, and often not the most interesting one.

This doesn't diminish writing. It expands the definition. The core skill—structuring thought for an audience—remains. But the forms multiply, and students should learn to work across them.

## Don't Retreat—Raise the Bar

The temptation is to simplify: ban AI, return to handwritten exams, treat the technology as a threat to be contained. But AI doesn't lower rigor—it demands more of it. Students must go deeper, design better questions, validate outputs, and build systems rather than just answers.

The risk is shallow AI use—students who let the system think for them and never develop judgment of their own. The opportunity is more ambitious intellectual work than was previously possible. A student who can design a multi-stage research workflow, ground it in carefully curated sources, critically evaluate every output, and synthesize the results into original argument is doing harder work than we've ever asked of undergraduates.

## The Structural Mismatch

Here's the underlying tension: institutions are still organized around individual authorship, static outputs, and assessment of finished products. But reality is shifting toward distributed cognition, iterative processes, and system-mediated thinking. The gap between how we evaluate and how people actually work is growing wider every semester.

This isn't just an assessment problem. It's a structural one. Grading rubrics, honor codes, degree requirements, tenure criteria—all assume a model of intellectual production that is rapidly becoming historical. Higher education hasn't caught up, and the longer it waits, the more painful the adjustment will be.

## The Proposal

If you compress everything: writing education should shift from teaching students to produce texts to teaching them to design, direct, and evaluate systems of thought. The writer becomes the architect of inquiry—setting direction, curating context, managing process, and taking responsibility for the result. Not a diminished role. A harder one.
