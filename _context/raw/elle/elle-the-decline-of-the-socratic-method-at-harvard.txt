Elle's notes:
Christopher Columbus Langdell introduced the Socratic method at Harvard Law School in 1870 as a way to make legal instruction more "scientific." For over a century, cold-calling and interactive oral questioning defined Harvard Law pedagogy—professors would interrogate students about cases, probing understanding through live dialogue. Kerr's 2014 study documents the method's decline: interviewing HLS faculty, he categorized them as "traditionalists" (true Socratic method), "quasi-traditionalists" (hybrid), and "counter-traditionalists" (expressly reject Socratic paradigm). The traditional method "still common during the 1950s and 1960s is nearly extinct." Yet even in decline, Socratic questioning remains more prominent at Harvard Law than undergraduate Harvard, where lecture-and-essay dominates.

Harvard relevance: Harvard Law pioneered oral pedagogy in America 156 years ago—yet Harvard College largely abandoned it. The Socratic method is fundamentally oral assessment as continuous practice: every class session tests whether students can articulate legal reasoning under pressure. Law professors recognized that written case briefs don't verify understanding—you need live dialogue to see if students can apply principles to novel scenarios. Kerr documents the method's "decline," but that decline is relative—HLS still uses oral questioning far more than undergraduate courses. The question: if Christopher Columbus Langdell (HLS Dean 1870-1895) believed oral dialogue was essential for rigorous legal training, why did his undergraduate contemporaries abandon it? The answer (per Cycles 1-2): scale and standardization, not pedagogy.

The Decline of the Socratic Method at Harvard
Orin S. Kerr, Nebraska Law Review, Vol. 78, Issue 1 (2014)

The author argues that "the traditional Socratic method is today more myth than reality" because legal pedagogy has fundamentally transformed. What was common in the 1950s-60s has become nearly obsolete.

Kerr categorizes Harvard professors into three groups:
- Traditionalists: derive their approach from classic Socratic methods
- Quasi-traditionalists: blend Socratic elements with significant innovations
- Counter-traditionalists: explicitly reject the Socratic paradigm

The paper explores why this decline occurred by analyzing faculty interviews and teaching styles, then suggests this evidence should reshape how we understand contemporary debates about legal education methods.
