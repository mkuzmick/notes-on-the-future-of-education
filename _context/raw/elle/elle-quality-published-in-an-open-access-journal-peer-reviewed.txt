3. Oral Communication in the Disciplines: A Framework for Faculty Development
:page_facing_up: Oral Communication in the Disciplines: A Resource for Teacher Development and Training
Deanna P. Dannels | Parlor Press, 2019
Summary: Dannels' book synthesizes decades of Communication Across the Curriculum (CAC) research to provide a framework for teaching oral communication in disciplinary contexts. The core argument: oral communication isn't a generic skill that transfers automatically across disciplines. Engineers, historians, and literary critics have different oral genres (technical briefings vs. seminar discussions vs. conference papers), norms (data-driven vs. interpretive), and values (objectivity vs. positionality). Effective oral communication pedagogy must be discipline-specific, not one-size-fits-all.
Harvard Context: Dannels' work challenges the idea that a single "Oral Communication" requirement (analogous to Expos for writing) would solve Harvard's assessment problems. Instead, it suggests that each concentration should define oral competencies for its discipline and scaffold them progressively. What does "speaking well" mean in Economics vs. History & Literature vs. Molecular & Cellular Biology? The answers differ substantially. For oral exams to be meaningful, they should reflect disciplinary practices: economists might defend policy proposals under questioning; historians might synthesize historiographical debates; scientists might present experimental design and defend methodology. This requires faculty development: most Harvard faculty have never been trained to teach or assess oral communication. Dannels' book could serve as a resource for departmental workshops on designing discipline-specific oral assignments.
Source quality: Scholarly monograph from a reputable press, authored by a leading CAC researcher. Highly credible.

About This Book

Oral Communication in the Disciplines: A Resource for Teacher Development and Training is divided into three sections. The first section provides a rationale for using oral communication as an instructional tool in various courses and briefly summarizes the strategic framework for the book. This section also identifies two kinds of oral communication assignments/activities, each of which address different instructional outcomes—informal communication activities to facilitate interrogation of course content, and more formal communication activities designed to explicitly develop communication competence. Each of the chapters includes decision-making templates and a number of examples of activities and assignments from various disciplines that could support these goals. The second section addresses student performance complexities unique to oral communication assignments and activities (e.g., managing apprehension, facilitating discussion, navigating group/team work, dealing with difficult interactions, and addressing diversity). Each of these chapters draws on communication research and provides pedagogical strategies for helping students manage the complexities when they emerge. The third section of the book discusses evaluation of oral communication assignments, feedback, and general assessment concerns related to evaluating oral communication. This section provides various strategies for creating rubrics, providing responses, and giving feedback on oral communication activities and assignments.
