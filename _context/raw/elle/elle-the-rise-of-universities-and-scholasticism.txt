Elle's notes:
The medieval university curriculum was fundamentally oral. Three pedagogical methods:
• Lectio — reading and commenting on authoritative texts (Aristotle, the Bible)
• Disputatio — "debating questions and arguments to sharpen reasoning skills"
• Quaestio — resolving contradictions through logical analysis
Students demonstrated understanding through "oral lectures and disputations" — notes (reportationes) and participation. The Master's degree required teaching in oral formats.

Why it matters: The modern university's very DNA is oral. The Bachelor's, Master's, and Doctoral degrees all originated from systems where speaking was the primary mode of intellectual demonstration. Writing came later.

and culture17.3 The transition from medieval to early modern Europeprint guidereport errorMedieval universities revolutionized education in Europe. Starting in the 12th century, these institutions emerged from cathedral and monastic schools, offering advanced studies in theology, law, and medicine. They attracted students from across the continent, fostering intellectual growth and cultural exchange.

The Catholic Church heavily influenced university development, providing funding and oversight. Universities trained clergy, defended orthodoxy, and expanded knowledge through scholasticism. This educational system shaped medieval thought, preserving classical learning while developing new ideas that would influence European culture for centuries to come.

The Rise of Universities in Medieval Europe

Emergence of medieval universities

- Early medieval schools laid the foundation for universities

- Cathedral schools provided education for clergy in urban centers (Chartres, Reims)

- Monastic schools offered education in rural monasteries (Monte Cassino, Cluny)

- 11th-12th centuries witnessed increased demand for education due to

- Growth of towns and trade requiring literate individuals for administration

- Need for educated clergy to serve the expanding Catholic Church

- First universities emerged in the 12th century

- University of Bologna (1088) focused on legal studies (Roman law, canon law)

- University of Paris (c. 1150) emphasized theology and philosophy (scholasticism)

- University of Oxford (c. 1167) initially focused on liberal arts and theology

- 13th century marked rapid expansion of universities across Europe

- Supported by the Catholic Church seeking to train clergy and maintain orthodoxy

- Secular rulers recognized the prestige and economic benefits of universities (Naples, Siena)

- 14th-15th centuries saw further growth and diversification of universities

- Establishment of universities in various European cities (Prague, Vienna, Heidelberg)

- Expansion of curricula to include medicine, mathematics, and natural sciences (Montpellier, Padua)

Catholic Church's university influence

- The Church recognized the need for educated clergy to defend faith and administer sacraments

- Universities trained theologians to counter heresies and interpret scriptures

- Canon lawyers were educated to administer church courts and legal matters

- Papal bulls granted universities legal and academic privileges

- Right to award degrees recognized throughout Christendom (licentia ubique docendi)

- Exemption from local jurisdiction, allowing academic freedom and mobility

- The Church provided financial support to universities

- Benefices and endowments funded professorships and scholarships

- Funding for construction of buildings and libraries (Sorbonne, Collegio di Spagna)

- Clergy served as professors and administrators, ensuring adherence to Church doctrine

- Bishops and abbots often served as chancellors overseeing university affairs

- Mendicant orders (Dominicans, Franciscans) played a significant role in universities

- The Church oversaw curriculum and teaching to maintain orthodoxy

- Emphasis on theology and philosophy to provide a Christian worldview

- Censorship of heretical ideas and works deemed contrary to Church teachings

Scholasticism and University Education

Medieval university curriculum

- Liberal arts curriculum formed the foundation of medieval university education

- Trivium focused on language and communication skills (grammar, rhetoric, logic)

- Quadrivium covered mathematical and scientific subjects (arithmetic, geometry, music, astronomy)

- Higher faculties offered advanced studies in specialized fields

- Theology was the most prestigious faculty, training clergy and exploring religious doctrines

- Law faculties taught canon law (church law) and civil law (Roman law)

- Medicine faculties provided training in human anatomy, physiology, and treatment of diseases

- Scholastic method dominated teaching and learning in medieval universities

- Lectio involved reading and commenting on authoritative texts (Bible, Church Fathers, Aristotle)

- Disputatio encouraged debating questions and arguments to sharpen reasoning skills

- Quaestio aimed at resolving contradictions and reaching conclusions through logical analysis

- Oral lectures and disputations were the primary modes of instruction

- Students took notes (reportationes) and participated in discussions to demonstrate understanding

- Examinations and degrees marked progress and achievement in university studies

- Bachelor's degree awarded after completing the liberal arts curriculum

- Master's degree granted after further study and teaching in a higher faculty

- Doctoral degree reserved for advanced scholars who made original contributions to their field

Impact of universities on knowledge

- Universities became centers of learning and scholarship in medieval Europe

- Attracted students and scholars from across Europe, facilitating the exchange of ideas

- Provided access to libraries and manuscript collections for study and research

- Scholasticism encouraged critical thinking and rational inquiry in various disciplines

- Application of logic and reason to theological and philosophical questions (existence of God, nature of the soul)

- Development of new ideas and theories in law, medicine, and natural philosophy

- Universities contributed to the preservation and transmission of classical knowledge

- Translation and study of Greek and Arabic texts (Aristotle, Galen, Avicenna)

- Integration of ancient learning with Christian thought (Thomism, Averroism)

- Universities fostered intellectual debate and controversy, challenging established ideas

- Disagreements between different schools of thought (realists vs. nominalists, Thomists vs. Scotists)

- Challenges to Church authority and traditional beliefs (Ockham, Wycliffe, Hus)

- Universities played a role in the development of vernacular literature and national identities

- Scholars began writing in their native languages (Dante, Chaucer, Christine de Pizan)

- Dissemination of knowledge beyond the Latin-speaking elite to wider audiences
