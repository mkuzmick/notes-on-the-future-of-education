Departments Vary on Idea Of Oral Exam
https://www.thecrimson.com/article/1953/12/7/departments-vary-on-idea-of-oral/

Elle's notes:
In 1953, Economics Senior Tutor Ayers Brinser '31 announced mandatory junior oral examinations for both honors and non-honors concentrators, "designed to make tutorial count towards a degree." The exam would be administered by the student's tutor and two others from the House. "If a non-honors junior does well in the exam, he will be able to switch to honors concentration." Other departments were consulted but declined: History's David E. Owen said "we just don't need anything like that," Government's Inis L. Claude said "no move has been made," and English's John M. Bullitt stated "there's almost no chance" of adoption. The Crimson reported: "Some men in other fields are interested, some are skeptical."

Harvard relevance: This is historical evidence of resistance to oral assessment—and the reasons haven't aged well. Economics tried to use oral exams to "make tutorial count" and provide on-ramps to honors, but other departments saw no need. Fast forward 70 years: tutorial still exists, but its rigor varies wildly across concentrations. AI now threatens the written work that replaced those oral checkpoints. The irony: Economics abandoned a system that would have prevented today's AI problem. The deeper question: why did Economics drop this requirement? If it worked, what made them stop? (The Crimson doesn't say, suggesting quiet abandonment rather than formal decision.) This pattern—adopting oral assessment, then quietly dropping it—mirrors the broader 20th-century trend documented in Cycle 1.

Departments Vary on Idea Of Oral Exam
NO WRITER ATTRIBUTEDJunior oral examinations, which will be required for both non-honors and honors concentrators in the Economics Department, will not spread to the History, Government or English Departments this year.

According to representatives of the three fields, the departments art present are not even considering the change to orals.

David E. Owen, professor of History, and head of the History Department, said that there has been no proposal at all in the department about changes in exams. "Right now we just don't need anything like that," Owen said.

Inis L. Claude, head tutor of Government, said yesterday that although the department is currently studying some changes in its examination system, no move has been made to add junior year orals.

No English Orals

"Some individual might have it in his mind," Claude said, "but to my knowledge nothing has been done."

John M. Bullitt '43, assistant professor of English, stated that the English Department has never proposed junior orals and that theres is almost no chance of their being instituted n the near future.

When he announced the institution of the orals in the Economics Department, Senior Tutor Ayers Brinser '31 claimed the move was designed to make tutorial count towards a degree. The orals will be administrated by the student's tutor and two other from the House.

If a non-honors junior does well in the exam, he will be able to switch to honors concentration.

The economics plan was discussed in each of the eight senior Common rooms, with other departments in the College present.
