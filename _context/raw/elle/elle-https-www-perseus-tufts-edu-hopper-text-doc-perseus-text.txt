The Hidden Barrier: Most Faculty Never Learned to Conduct Oral Exams
Here's an uncomfortable truth about expanding oral assessment at Harvard: most professors earned their PhDs through written dissertations, published their way to tenure through journal articles, and were hired based on research productivity—not oral examination skills. Research from the UK's Higher Education Academy shows that fewer than 15% of faculty receive any formal training in conducting oral assessments, yet we expect them to design fair rubrics, ask probing follow-up questions, manage anxiety, and evaluate spontaneous responses consistently across dozens of students. The result is wildly uneven implementation: some faculty conduct rigorous Socratic dialogues, others default to superficial "tell me about your paper" conversations that reward performative confidence over genuine understanding.
If Harvard seriously pursues oral assessment in the AI era, faculty development must come first—not as an afterthought. This means Bok Center workshops on question design (open-ended prompts that surface reasoning, not factual recall), calibration sessions where faculty practice scoring sample oral responses together, and mentorship pairing experienced oral examiners (from Social Studies, History & Lit, or professional schools) with faculty new to the format. Law schools have decades of experience training adjuncts to conduct oral arguments; medical schools run annual OSCE examiner training. Harvard College needs equivalent infrastructure. Without it, we risk reproducing existing inequities: faculty who already conduct tutorials comfortably will expand oral work, while those in large lecture courses avoid it entirely, creating a two-tier system where oral skill development depends on which concentration you choose.

advance-he.ac.ukHome | Advance HEAdvance HE - We help higher education organisations be the best they can be, by unlocking the potential of their people.[8:50 AM]Single-Point Rubrics Preserve Authenticity in Oral Assessment
Most faculty default to analytic rubrics for oral exams—grids with criteria like "clarity," "organization," and "depth" rated on 1-5 scales. Research shows this approach backfires: students learn to perform rubric compliance rather than demonstrate genuine understanding, and evaluators struggle with "halo effects" where one strong/weak dimension colors all scores. Worse, analytic rubrics force faculty to quantify inherently qualitative judgments ("Is this response a 3 or a 4 on 'engagement with counterarguments'?"), producing false precision that masks subjective interpretation. Single-point rubrics offer a better alternative: describe the expected competency in one column, then provide space for feedback on what exceeded/fell short of that standard. Instead of rating "thesis defense" 1-5, you write: "Student explained thesis clearly but struggled when pressed on alternative interpretations."
Harvard faculty adopting oral assessment should resist the temptation to create elaborate scoring matrices that promise objectivity but deliver performativity. Single-point rubrics keep evaluation focused on holistic reasoning rather than checklist compliance, they're faster to complete (critical when conducting 20+ oral exams per course), and they generate more actionable feedback for students. The trade-off: they require trusting faculty judgment rather than hiding behind numerical aggregation. But if we don't trust professors to evaluate student reasoning qualitatively, we shouldn't be conducting oral exams in the first place—the entire pedagogical case rests on faculty expertise in recognizing understanding through dialogue. Train examiners, calibrate standards, but don't sacrifice authentic assessment for the illusion of objectivity.

When Harvard faculty look enviously at Oxford's weekly tutorial system—where every student meets 1:1 or 1:2 with their tutor to discuss essays and defend arguments orally—they often miss the institutional infrastructure that makes it possible. Oxford operates at a 10:1 student-faculty ratio (vs. Harvard's 7:1), but crucially, tutorials are the primary mode of instruction, not an add-on. Faculty teach 8-10 hours of tutorials per week instead of lecturing; students write weekly essays specifically for tutorial discussion rather than for grading. The entire academic calendar, workload expectations, and faculty reward structure revolves around intensive oral pedagogy. Harvard's lecture-plus-section model serves different goals: broader course coverage, research productivity incentives, and economies of scale.
The lesson for Harvard isn't to replicate Oxford wholesale—that would require dismantling core features of the American research university—but to learn selectively. Oxford demonstrates that regular low-stakes oral work (weekly tutorials) makes high-stakes oral exams (finals, vivas) feel natural rather than terrifying. Students develop "tutorial voice"—the ability to think aloud, revise arguments mid-sentence, and defend positions under questioning—through habitual practice, not one-shot assessment. Harvard could adapt this principle without full tutorials: make section participation more structured and cumulative, require brief oral "check-ins" every two weeks where students explain their evolving thesis, or pilot tutorial-style small groups in junior seminars. The goal: make oral intellectual performance ordinary, not exceptional. When oral dialogue is woven into pedagogy rather than reserved for assessment, it stops feeling like a gatekeeping ritual and starts feeling like thinking itself.

The Irony of Using AI to Evaluate "AI-Proof" Oral Exams
Several universities are piloting AI-assisted oral assessment systems: automated transcription for record-keeping, LLM-generated follow-up questions, and even GPT-4 scoring oral responses against rubrics. The pitch is compelling—reduce faculty workload, ensure consistency, enable asynchronous evaluation. But research from fairness audits reveals troubling patterns: speech recognition systems show 19-35% higher error rates for non-native accents (Koenecke et al., Stanford 2020), LLM evaluators favor verbose responses over concise ones regardless of content quality, and automated scoring systems struggle with the very qualities oral exams should value—spontaneity, revision of thinking mid-sentence, and authentic struggle with complex ideas.
The deeper irony: if we adopt oral assessment to combat AI-generated writing, then use AI to evaluate oral performance, we've simply shifted the automation problem rather than solved it. Harvard should be skeptical of "AI-assisted grading" for oral work, but transcription for accessibility (providing students with records of their performance for review) and question banks (not auto-generated, but curated by faculty) can support rather than replace human judgment. The line matters: tools that augment faculty expertise preserve pedagogy; tools that automate evaluation undermine the entire rationale for oral assessment. If an algorithm can score oral responses reliably, those responses probably aren't testing what we think they are. Keep humans in the loop—that's the whole point of choosing oral work in the first place.

The opening passage presents Socrates encountering Phaedrus outside Athens's walls. Socrates asks, "Whither away, and where do you come from?" Phaedrus explains he has spent the morning with Lysias, son of Cephalus, and now walks the roads for exercise on medical advice.

This introductory dialogue establishes the conversational setting. Phaedrus carries a speech manuscript from Lysias, which will become the catalyst for the philosophical discussion that unfolds throughout the work. The countryside setting, away from the city proper, creates an intimate atmosphere suitable for intellectual exchange about rhetoric, love, and the nature of the soul.
