Early Curriculum at Harvard: Historical Sources
https://guides.library.harvard.edu/hua/earlycurriculum

Elle's notes:
This archival research guide reveals that Harvard's original curriculum was fundamentally oral. Key details:
• Latin was the language of instruction (requirement not renewed after 1692)
• "Commencement theses and quaestiones" (disputations) were central to academic life
• Student assessment involved thesis disputations supervised by each president
• The 1643 pamphlet New Englands First Fruits documents the curriculum's oral examination structure

Why it matters: Harvard wasn't founded as a written-exam institution—it was founded as an oral one. The disputation tradition was core to the mission of "advancing Learning and perpetuating it to Posterity." Any return to oral assessment isn't a novelty; it's a homecoming.

Introduction

 

The Harvard University Archives supports the University's mission of education and research by collecting, preserving and providing access to an accurate, authentic, and complete record of the life of the University. The Archives’ collections reflect the emergence of higher education in the United States, tracing the development of academic disciplines and pedagogical trends, in addition to highlighting the history of intellectual and social life at Harvard and beyond.
