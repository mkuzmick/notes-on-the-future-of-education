The article quotes Tricia Bertram Gallant, director of UC San Diego's Academic Integrity Office: "Oral assessments are definitely experiencing a renaissance." Examples include University of Wyoming professor Catherine Hartmann, who redesigned courses to culminate in individual oral exams—students sit in her office for half-hour conversations rather than submitting essays. The trend began during the pandemic (cheating fears in online environments) and accelerated post-2023 with ChatGPT.

Harvard relevance: Harvard isn't alone—this is a national movement. The Post documents faculty at Wyoming, UCSD, and multiple institutions independently arriving at the same solution. The pattern suggests oral assessment isn't a fringe experiment but an emerging best practice. Importantly, the article frames this as faculty adaptation, not institutional mandate. Professors are making tactical decisions to preserve learning integrity, not waiting for policy committees. Harvard faculty have the autonomy to do likewise. The "renaissance" framing is also strategic: it positions oral exams as recovery of tradition, not desperate innovation.
