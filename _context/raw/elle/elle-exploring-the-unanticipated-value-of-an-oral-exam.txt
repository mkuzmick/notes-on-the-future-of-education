Elle's notes:
A February 2025 study examined Integrated Oral Examinations (IOE) that test material across multiple courses simultaneously. Findings: "The IOE has both a summative and formative effect (ie, Assessment of Learning and Assessment for Learning) and also functions as a high-impact assessment practice." The study notes: "Compared to their written counterpart, oral assessments provide a wealth of information about student understanding. Instead of deciphering a static response, oral assessments provide instructors the opportunity to probe a student's understanding in real time." The IOE format helps students "integrate knowledge across courses" rather than compartmentalize learning by subject.

Harvard relevance: This addresses a core liberal arts goal: synthesis across disciplines. Harvard's General Education program aims to develop "breadth and connected learning"—but traditional course-by-course written exams encourage siloed knowledge (students study for Econ 10 separately from Gov 20). Integrated oral exams could test whether students can connect economic theory to political institutions, literary analysis to historical context, etc. The "high-impact practice" designation is significant—these are pedagogies proven to increase retention and deep learning. For Harvard's tutorial system: joint oral examinations across related courses (e.g., History & Literature's interdisciplinary model) could replace separate written finals, forcing students to demonstrate integrative thinking. The formative + summative finding also matters: oral exams aren't just end-of-course gatekeeping—they teach students how to synthesize through the preparation process.

Abstract

Background
An oral examination, in addition to assessing knowledge gains, can assess various skills including communication, critical thinking, and decision-making. These anticipated outcomes are valuable in helping students matriculate. This study aims to describe and explore unanticipated effects of an oral exam, and how those effects can have a positive long-term influence on student learning.

Educational activity
An Integrated Oral Exam (IOE) was used to assess a student's ability to effectively integrate and apply knowledge from multiple pharmacotherapy content areas to address complex patient cases. Students were required to develop care plans, and verbally explain, rationalize, and defend the plans with an evaluator during a one-on-one question and answer assessment. The IOE is administered in the spring semester of the second year. It consists of two cases followed by a debriefing session using a modified plus-delta approach.

Evaluation findings
An evaluation sought to develop a deeper understanding of the exam by exploring: why is this working and what is happening? Five years of student debriefing documentation underwent multiple cycles of coding. In addition to inductive coding exploring the exam's functioning, Fink's Taxonomy of Significant Learning was used for deductive coding.

Analysis of educational activity
While it was expected that there would be evidence of the exam's influence on Foundational Knowledge and Integration, unanticipated exam effects included Caring, Learning How to Learn, and the Human Dimension from Fink's Taxonomy. The IOE has both a summative and formative effect (ie, Assessment of Learning and an Assessment for Learning) and also functions as a high-impact assessment practice.

Summary

Evidence was identified supporting the assessment's influence on Foundational Knowledge, Integration, Caring, the Human Dimension (ie, learning something important about oneself), and Learning How to Learn.
