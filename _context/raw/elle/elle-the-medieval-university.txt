Elle's notes:
Cambridge's official institutional history documents that for 500+ years, oral disputation was not supplemental—it was the assessment. Medieval examinations were "oral disputations in which the candidates advanced a series of questions or theses which they disputed or argued with opponents a little senior to themselves, and finally with the masters who had taught them." Teaching took the form of "reading and explaining texts"—but learning was verified through live, adversarial debate. Students couldn't memorize; they had to defend positions under cross-examination from peers and faculty. This was the universitas model: knowledge as communal, dialogic, performative.

Harvard relevance: For the first 500 years of European university history, oral examination wasn't a choice among formats—it was the format. Harvard's 1636 founding inherited this tradition directly (as documented in Theme 2). The question isn't "Should we try oral exams?" but "Why did we abandon what worked for half a millennium?" Understanding this history reframes the AI conversation: we're not proposing innovation, we're recovering tradition. Medieval universities trusted oral disputation because you can't fake understanding in real-time argument. AI hasn't changed that fundamental truth.

The Medieval University at Cambridge

The page describes how early Cambridge students organized their academic pursuits following patterns established in Italy, France, and Oxford.

Curriculum and Degrees

Students began with a "foundation course" in arts covering grammar, logic, and rhetoric, progressing to arithmetic, music, geometry, and astronomy before earning bachelor and master degrees. Advanced students could pursue divinity, canon and civil law, or medicine.

Teaching and Examination

"The teaching took the form of reading and explaining texts; the examinations were oral disputations in which the candidates advanced a series of questions or theses."

Academic Structure

Masters conducted teaching after completing their own studies and receiving collegial approval. Advanced scholars became doctors and organized into specific faculties. The Chancellor, deputized by a Vice-Chancellor, conferred degrees on behalf of the entire academic body.

Administrative Development

The University introduced matriculation (enrollment on a master's roll) to authenticate degree recipients. Regent Masters elected Proctors to represent them and manage affairs. Additional officers—including Bedells, Chaplains, Registraries, and Orators—gradually emerged to handle ceremonies, records, and correspondence.

Governance

"Statutes were adopted by the whole body of the University" to address emerging problems, initially recorded haphazardly by Proctors before systematic documentation began in the mid-thirteenth century.
