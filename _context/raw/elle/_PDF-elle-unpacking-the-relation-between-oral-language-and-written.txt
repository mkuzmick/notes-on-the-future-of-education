Unpacking the Relation Between Oral Language and Written Composition
https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.3102/00346543251320359

Elle's notes:
A January 2025 meta-analysis examined the relationship between oral language and written composition, finding that "the relation between oral language and written composition strengthened as students progressed through grade levels and was more pronounced among foreign language learners compared to native speakers." This suggests oral fluency and writing ability are mutually reinforcing, not competing skills. The meta-analysis also found oral language competency predicts writing quality—meaning students who can articulate ideas verbally are better writers. Conestoga College's Faculty Learning Hub (March 2025) notes: "Students may study harder for an in-person test than for a written test" (Iannone & Simpson 2015, Zhao 2018, Hazen 2020).

Harvard relevance: This refutes the false dichotomy that "oral assessment replaces writing instruction." Instead, oral fluency strengthens writing ability—the skills are complementary. For Harvard: integrating oral assessment doesn't mean abandoning writing; it means developing both competencies in tandem. The finding that foreign language learners show stronger oral-written correlations is particularly relevant for Harvard's ~12% international undergraduate population and domestic multilingual students. The "students study harder for oral exams" finding (replicated across three studies) suggests oral assessment increases student effort, not just verifies learning. This addresses the "rigor" concern—if students prepare more intensively for oral exams, they learn more deeply.
