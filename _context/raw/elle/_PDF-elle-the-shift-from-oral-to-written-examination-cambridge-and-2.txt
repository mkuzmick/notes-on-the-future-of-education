Ian Gilbert explainshttps://www.independentthinking.co.uk/resources/a-brief-history-of-the-written-exam/[8:18 AM]Christopher Stray's 2001 historical research pinpoints the mechanism: Cambridge led the shift because Newtonian mathematics dominated its curriculum. Written exams worked well for mathematics—problems have right answers, showing your work matters, and calculation can be verified on paper. This success generalized to other disciplines even where oral assessment might have been superior. By contrast, Oxford retained oral exams (vivas) into the 20th century for humanities subjects. Stray notes that by the 1850s, Cambridge's vivas "had become rehearsed exhibitions"—theater rather than genuine assessment—while written tests offered apparent objectivity.

Harvard relevance: The history reveals contingency, not inevitability. Written exams won at Cambridge not because they were universally better, but because math departments pioneered them and the model spread. Other fields adopted written assessment by imitation, not pedagogical necessity. This matters now: if humanities, social sciences, and qualitative fields never needed written exams—if they adopted them for institutional conformity—then AI's disruption of written work isn't a crisis for these disciplines. It's permission to return to formats (oral argument, dialogue, defense) that may always have been more authentic for non-quantitative subjects.
