“With word and deed we insert ourselves into the human world, and this insertion is like a second birth, in which we confirm and take upon ourselves the naked fact of our original physical appearance. This insertion is not forced upon us by necessity, like labor, and is not prompted by utility, like work. It may be stimulated by the presence of others whose company we may wish to join, but it is never conditioned by them; its impulse springs from the beginning which came into the world when we were born and to which we respond by beginning something new on our own initiative.”

Hannah Arendt, The Human Condition (Chicago: The University of Chicago Press, 1958), 176–177.

Speech (an exercise of the right to be seen and heard), plurality, and natality, are core concepts in Arendt’s work that are experienced in the Hannah Arendt Center (HAC) Dialogue Project groups. Speaking in small group dialogue is one way to exercise the right to be seen and heard that can reinforce our ability and determination to speak out in other settings. To “think what we are doing” (Arendt 1958, 5) is one of Arendt’s fundamental recommendations. The HAC’s success in drawing people from many parts of the globe into the Virtual Reading Group (VRG) has meant that there is a wide range of perspectives entering into the VRG sessions, as well as in the HAC Dialogue Groups. For Arendt, sitting around a table establishes a space of equality in which people can appear, that is, be seen and heard. One might say Arendt is the table, offering her readers a shared reality, a common world in which to explore today’s crucial political and social questions.

Hannah Arendt was prescient about the increasing alienation in the modern world due to technology. Perhaps she could not have imagined the opposite effect, that of connection, taking place in zoom conversations among people all around the world. In 2014 The Hannah Arendt Center at Bard began to use a virtual platform for a reading group. The classroom format of the Virtual Reading Group included a lecture and Q & A with Roger Berkowitz, which initially drew about 25–35 people each week. In 2020, during the height of the Covid pandemic, the numbers grew exponentially to around 100 each week (and have remained at that level since). In 2021 the HAC Dialogue Project began offering additional opportunities for Arendt readers to share their perspectives on her work, and to explore her concepts to help us understand current political conditions.

Plurality as the basis of the political realm is a major thread in Arendt’s thought: hearing from as many viewpoints as possible allows us to make better judgments. Arendt opposes the notion that thinking is done only in solitude, with the argument that after one withdraws from the world (even there we carry other voices with us), we must come back into the world to think what we are doing, with others. Staying open to the influence of others, a core principle in Martin Buber’s belief in dialogue, is not just listening, but working to understand, what he describes as meeting on a narrow ridge between I and Thou. Suspending judgment until we have done the work to understand, is a centerpiece of David Bohm’s model of dialogue. Plurality as Arendt defines it, is equality in the political sphere–the right to be seen and heard. What Arendt does not explain is that plurality rests on goodwill. She acknowledges that Jasper’s work identifies goodwill as the basis for all communication, but then seems to assume it will occur. Goodwill requires equal respect for all participants, and demonstration of the willingness and ability to stay open as one listens. We may like to think we are always doing this, but these skills are not often taught or valued in our current culture. Like all skills, these are learned, and are strengthened by practice. Martin Buber and Carl Rogers, among others, spent their lives practicing dialogue.

Finally, Arendt’s concept of natality, the initiation of something new in the world which happens when we are born, and again when we take our place in the world as political actors, is a crucial element in dialogue. While no one is required to speak, there is a space for all who want to speak, and a necessity for enough people to speak that many viewpoints are represented. Standing one’s ground, holding a point of view, is essential for plurality — in order for others to “go visiting” as Arendt describes it. You can’t go visiting if there’s no one home. Thus, we need to create spaces of courage — of natality — where ideas can be expressed and shared and thoughtfully critiqued, rather than setting a goal of creating safe spaces. Dialogue is not about “compromise.” Speech as a form of action, requires courage. Dialogue does not mean absence of conflict. If each stands their ground, there will be endless differences. Using conflict as an opportunity to gain clarity about what we believe, points us toward what we must do, or not do. Creative conflict may sound like an oxymoron, and indeed it may be a seldom seen phenomenon, but welcoming conflict instead of fearing and avoiding it, allows us to deepen understanding and make better judgments.

The Hannah Arendt Center Dialogue Project is now in its fifth year. At a time of rapidly escalating levels of uncertainty, in both politics and the climate, offering a space to think together what we are doing, has proved to be an oasis for many participants. With goodwill as a reference point for engaging in dialogue, participants must determine whether they can maintain goodwill towards others who may have different perspectives and life experiences, and they likewise must assess whether others show goodwill toward them. The dialogue materials, three 1-page documents, explain what people are being asked to do — not just to be polite or civil, but to listen to understand what others are thinking and experiencing, and to speak to be understood. Participation is based on maintaining respect for all and sharing responsibility for the well-being of the whole group, essentially a leaderless model. Goodwill is not based on political opinions. If someone loses goodwill they are expected to withdraw — perhaps to find others who see things as they do, or just to take a break. Goodwill can be lost but it can also be regained. Deciding to withdraw does not make one “the enemy;” it leaves the door open to return.

Sessions begin with small breakout groups in which each person has about three minutes to speak uninterrupted about their thoughts related to the reading. The reading may be from Arendt’s work or that of other thinkers. There is time after the go-round to ask questions or comment about what was said. There are several reasons the format begins with small groups. Some participants may have so much to say it can be difficult to listen to others. Having a chance to speak can make room to listen. Others may be hesitant to speak in a large group, or tend to give authority to those more confident in their opinions, with the consequence that they don’t speak at all. After the small groups, the whole group reconvenes and the member who selected the reading makes a brief presentation, which is followed by a discussion.

Questions remain about whether public dialogue is political as Arendt defines it; in some places she says speech itself is political, in others it seems she would require that some decision regarding the public good must be at stake. The dialogue groups are providing a place for Arendt readers to engage with her work and that of other thinkers. We are experiencing some of the most basic concepts Arendt presents: plurality, natality, and the right to be seen and heard. We are resisting the pervasive loneliness in our world, at the same time we each navigate present challenges. The public dialogue model materials are made available so that it may be easily reproduced. As we are seeing deep divisions worldwide, as well as in the nation, our communities, and our families, history reminds us of the disastrous results from such chasms. It seems that war, the age-old solution to conflict, just goes on and on and on. Arendt hoped that thinking, as perhaps only she defined it, could prevent evil. Indeed, more than ever before, this idea is being put to the test.
