Exploring alternative assessments during COVID: Instructor experiences using oral exams in chemistry education

This article examines how two chemistry educators implemented oral exams as alternative summative assessments during the COVID-19 pandemic—one instructor in general chemistry and another in organic chemistry.

The study explores whether oral exams can provide "a more comprehensive evaluation of students' learning" compared to traditional written examinations alone.

Advantages of Oral Exams:
- Enable deeper probing of student comprehension through direct interaction
- Assess critical thinking and communication skills more effectively
- Create opportunities for novice-expert dialogue that makes learning visible
- Provide authentic professional experiences similar to workplace communication
- May benefit non-native English speakers and students with dyslexia or dyscalculia

Challenges Identified:
- Student anxiety about one-on-one evaluation format
- Potential for examiner bias without anonymity
- Variability in questioning between individual student sessions
- Time-intensive implementation for instructors

The instructors grounded their approach in constructivist learning theory, emphasizing that "learners themselves build their knowledge in their minds as they have new experiences." This framework justifies the use of guided dialogue to help students make meaningful connections.

The authors argue oral exams offer students authentic ways to develop professional chemistry identities while demonstrating expertise beyond memorization.
