Havelock showed that the Iliad functioned as an "oral encyclopedia"—not entertainment, but the primary educational technology for preserving cultural knowledge before widespread literacy. Plato's attack on poets (mimesis, emotional identification) was actually an attack on oral pedagogy itself—Plato wanted to replace embodied, performative, mnemonic oral learning with abstract, written, philosophical reasoning. Havelock argued that Plato's Republic represents the transition from oral to literate culture, with all its cognitive and social consequences.

Harvard relevance: Havelock provides historical depth to the oral/written debate. For most of human history, oral performance WAS education—the Iliad wasn't a text to analyze but a script to memorize, embody, and perform. When we say "oral exams test different things," Havelock shows what they test: embodied knowledge, performative recall, situational application—the cognitive skills of oral cultures. Modern education privileged writing because literacy enables abstraction, analysis, critique (Plato's project). But if AI now performs those literate functions, maybe we need to recover oral culture's emphasis on presence, dialogue, and adaptive performance. Havelock's key insight: oral knowledge isn't inferior—it's differently structured. Oral exams don't test "less rigor"—they test oral rigor (synthesis under pressure, responsive argumentation).
