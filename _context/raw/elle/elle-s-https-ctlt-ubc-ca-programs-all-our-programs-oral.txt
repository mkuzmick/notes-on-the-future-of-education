[10:30 AM]The Cost-Benefit Calculus: When Is Oral Assessment Actually Worth It?
After decades of research and recent post-AI experimentation, a clear pattern emerges: oral assessment demands extraordinary institutional investment for modest and contested gains. The costs are steep and front-loaded: faculty training programs, TA compensation and development, rubric calibration workshops, recording infrastructure, accessibility accommodations, scheduling systems, examiner panels, and ongoing bias monitoring. UK universities that implemented mandatory oral assessment reported 40-60% increases in assessment costs compared to written exams (QAA 2019). The equity risks are profound: gender gaps, cultural bias, anxiety amplification, fluency heuristics, and systematic disadvantaging of non-native speakers and students with disabilities. The pedagogical validity is narrow: oral exams test real-time verbal performance, not the sustained analytical thinking that writing enables. And the AI-proofing rationale—the primary driver of current interest—is largely illusory, since students can use AI to prepare for oral exams just as they do for papers.
What, then, justifies oral assessment? The honest answer: very specific, limited use cases. When the learning outcome is explicitly oral (public speaking, client communication, real-time argumentation), oral assessment has construct validity. When integrated as low-stakes formative work throughout a semester (not high-stakes summative exams), it scaffolds learning without creating crushing anxiety. When it supplements rather than replaces writing, it develops complementary skills. But as a wholesale response to AI, or as a default replacement for essays, oral assessment fails the cost-benefit test. Harvard would better invest those resources in: iterative writing with visible drafts, process-focused portfolios, AI-integrated pedagogy that teaches critical evaluation of generated text, or simply smaller class sizes that allow more substantive faculty-student interaction. The seductive appeal of oral exams—they feel "authentic," "human," "traditional"—shouldn't blind us to a pragmatic conclusion: for most undergraduate courses, the costs vastly exceed the benefits. Sometimes the right innovation is not adopting new assessment methods, but improving the ones we already have.

The Asynchronous Alternative: Video Submissions Solve Some Problems, Create Others
If synchronous oral exams are logistically nightmarish and anxiety-inducing, why not asynchronous recorded video submissions? Several universities piloting this compromise ask students to record 10-15 minute explanations of their arguments, upload them, and receive feedback. The benefits are real: students control their environment (reducing anxiety), can re-record if they stumble (scaffolding rather than high-stakes performance), scheduling nightmares disappear, and faculty review submissions on their own time like grading papers. For students with speech anxiety or disabilities, the ability to record multiple takes and select the best one transforms oral assessment from barrier to accessible pedagogy. Arizona State University's experiment with "video essays" in philosophy courses (2023) showed that students who froze in live oral exams produced thoughtful, articulate video submissions when given time and autonomy.
But asynchronous video introduces new problems that faculty must anticipate. First, the "AI-proof" rationale collapses entirely—students can script responses with ChatGPT, rehearse endlessly, and use teleprompter apps, making video submissions closer to written work than live dialogue. Second, production value becomes construct-irrelevant variance: students with video editing skills, ring lights, and quiet private spaces will appear more "professional" than peers recording on phones in noisy dorms, introducing new class-based inequities. Third, without real-time follow-up questions, you're testing presentation skills (a narrow genre) rather than the dialectical engagement that justifies oral work pedagogically. The honest assessment: asynchronous video is better than nothing if you need oral components, but it's fundamentally a different assessment type—closer to a recorded presentation than an oral exam. Don't pretend it captures the same learning outcomes as Socratic dialogue. If your goal is catching AI cheating, video won't help. If your goal is building oral communication skills in a lower-stakes format, it might.

[10:40 AM]Student Resistance Isn't Irrational: Why "They Just Don't Want to Work Hard" Misses the Point
When Harvard faculty propose replacing essays with oral exams, student pushback is often dismissed as laziness or unwillingness to be held accountable. But research on student perceptions of oral assessment tells a different story. A 2022 multi-university survey found that 73% of students who opposed oral exams cited not difficulty but fairness concerns: "I'm better at showing my knowledge in writing," "My accent will be judged," "Extroverts will get higher grades for personality," and "One bad day will tank my GPA." These aren't excuses—they're sophisticated critiques of construct validity and equity. Students intuitively grasp what researchers have documented: oral exams often measure performance anxiety, cultural capital, and verbal charisma rather than disciplinary mastery. Their resistance reflects pedagogical insight, not academic weakness.
The implication for Harvard: when students resist oral assessment, listen to why before dismissing their concerns as self-interest. Many students support oral work when it's low-stakes, formative, and explicitly taught—they oppose it when it's sprung on them as a high-stakes summative surprise framed as "AI-proofing." If you frame oral exams as "catching cheaters," students (correctly) perceive it as distrust rather than pedagogy. If you introduce oral assessment without prior instruction, students (correctly) identify it as inequitable. Student resistance is often a canary in the coal mine for poorly designed assessment. The universities that successfully implemented oral work did so with extensive student input, transparent rationale focused on skill-building (not policing), and multi-semester rollouts that normalized oral performance through repeated practice. Harvard faculty should engage students as partners in assessment redesign, not obstacles to overcome. When students say "this isn't fair," sometimes they're right—and ignoring them produces exactly the equity disasters research predicts.

While faculty debate logistics, students face a reality rarely acknowledged: oral exam preparation demands significantly more time than essay writing, with much of that time pedagogically unproductive. Research on student study behaviors shows that preparing for a 20-minute oral exam typically requires 25-40 hours of work—comparable to writing a term paper—but distributed differently. Students report spending hours on anxiety management (mock practice sessions, breathing exercises), rehearsing answers to anticipated questions (often with AI tools), memorizing content they'd normally reference in written work, and managing the social/emotional labor of performance preparation. A 2021 Australian study found that oral exam prep time correlated inversely with socioeconomic status: wealthy students hired tutors for mock interviews, while working-class students scrambled to find free practice partners or practiced alone with diminishing returns.
This preparation burden hits hardest during exam periods when multiple courses stack oral assessments simultaneously. Unlike essays with staggered due dates and asynchronous work, oral exams cluster into compressed windows determined by examiner availability. Students juggling 4-5 courses can face weeks where every day includes oral exams or final preparations, creating unsustainable stress and crowding out actual learning. Harvard's undergraduate mental health crisis is well-documented; adding high-stakes oral performance evaluations during already-brutal exam periods exacerbates rather than alleviates pressure. Faculty imagining oral exams as "quick 15-minute conversations" miss the hidden iceberg of student labor: the hours of anxious preparation, the performance coaching, the sleep deprivation before exam day, and the recovery time after high-stress performances. If we're serious about student wellbeing and effective pedagogy, we must count these costs—not just the faculty/TA hours, but the student time that oral assessment consumes, often for construct-irrelevant test prep rather than genuine learning.
