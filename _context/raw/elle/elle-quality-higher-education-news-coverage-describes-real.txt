Abstract
The emergence of generative artificial intelligence has forced humanities educators to reconsider traditional assessment methods, particularly take-home writing assignments vulnerable to content produced by generative artificial intelligence. This paper examines the implementation of oral exams in an upper-level humanities course as a response to generative AI. Using backward design principles, the author redesigned the entire course structure to support oral assessment, implementing technology-free classrooms, social annotation platforms, and systematic "cold calling" to prepare students for final oral examinations. The paper addresses common concerns about oral exams, including student anxiety, fairness, and instructor time management, through evidence-based strategies and careful implementation. The author found that oral exams not only effectively addressed AI-related academic integrity issues but also helped students develop critical communication skills. Students reported positive experiences and improved understanding of course material, while instructor time investment proved comparable to traditional paper grading. This research contributes to pedagogical literature by providing practical guidance for implementing oral exams in humanities courses, demonstrating their effectiveness as both an AI-resistant assessment method and a valuable tool for developing essential academic and professional skills. While acknowledging limitations, the study offers a viable alternative for upper-level humanities instruction in an increasingly AI-integrated educational landscape.
