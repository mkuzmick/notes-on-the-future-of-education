# The Two Hardest Practical Questions: Recording and Accommodations

Among the unanswered questions the meeting surfaced, two stand out because they cannot be solved by a better rubric, a longer workshop, or more TA hours. They require explicit institutional commitments and, in both cases, partnership with offices outside the Bok Center (General Counsel and Privacy on recording; the Disability Access Office on accommodations). Both came into view through concrete pilot experience. Craycraft's AI-augmented pilot in folklore and performance showed 86% of students felt preparation deepened their understanding, but only 55% would choose the format again — and the resistance landed on the recording and studio environment, not on the oral format itself. Kaminsky, in the same meeting, asked what transcripts and technology should actually be *for* — a question that runs through both halves of this report.

This is not a recommendation memo. It is a map of the live tensions, the places where the existing literature stops short, and the decisions the PCP team will have to make before scaling beyond a single pilot.

## 1. Recording — a designed variable, not a default

### The case for recording is not one case, it is four

Most institutional guides (Guelph, Princeton McGraw, Fenton 2025) fold all arguments for recording into a single sentence — usually some version of "record in case of grade appeals." That undersells the range of purposes recording serves, and it also undersells how different those purposes are in their privacy footprint. Four distinct rationales have been advanced for recording oral assessments, and each has a different half-life and a different access model.

1. **Grade appeals.** A contemporaneous rubric filled in during a fifteen-minute conversation is a fragile artifact compared to a written exam booklet. The appeal lives or dies on whether the grader's notes align with what the student actually said. Retention: until the appeal window closes (typically 30–60 days post-grade-posting). Access: student, instructor, dean on request.

2. **Inter-rater calibration.** The inter-rater reliability problem the Bok Graduate Fellows have flagged (0.4–0.6 for oral vs. 0.7–0.85 for written, with examiner variance exceeding student variance in the PMC1114733 medical study) is the single strongest psychometric objection to oral assessment. The standard mitigation — multi-examiner calibration and TF norming sessions using recorded practice exams — *requires* recording. Retention: as long as the norming set is in use. Access: instructor and TFs, for training only.

3. **Student self-review.** Pemantle's 4-point rubric and Kaminsky's structured self-assessment at the end of each session both presume the student has enough information to reflect. A recording lets the student hear "you know a lot of techniques but they seem jumbled" in their own voice, which is pedagogically richer than a numeric score. Retention: through the student's degree, at the student's option. Access: the student, by default.

4. **Pilot iteration and research.** Craycraft's pilot could not have yielded the 86%/55% gap finding without artifacts to review. Research use, however, requires a separate consent regime under Harvard's HRPP / IRB expectations, not the default capture most classroom policies imagine.

Conflating these four rationales is why students resist. A student who would happily consent to #1 (narrow, short retention, appeal-triggered) may refuse #4 (research, indefinite retention, broader access) — and the typical consent form does not let them.

### Why students resist — and why the pilot data already tells us this is not irrational

Three things make recording harder in an oral format than in, for example, a recorded lecture.

- **Anxiety compounds.** Oral assessment is already documented as higher-anxiety than written (Fenton 2025; Guelph guide). The camera adds a second audience — indefinite, deferred, and unknown. The student is now performing both for the instructor and for reviewers who may or may not ever watch.
- **FERPA's treatment of the recording is stronger than most faculty realize.** Once captured, the recording is an education record: access-controlled, disclosable only for legitimate educational interest, subject to the student's right of access and amendment, governed by the institution's retention schedule. "It's just for grading purposes" is a good-faith reassurance, not a FERPA-compliant policy. If the recording lives on Zoom Cloud, Panopto, Canvas Studio, or a departmental drive, the subprocessor chain matters — and most instructors cannot describe it.
- **ASR bias turns transcripts into a second injury.** CU Boulder (Ward & Southwell, 2025) documented 24% lower ASR accuracy for Black speakers than white speakers, and 46–56% word error rates for younger speakers whose speech the training corpus underrepresents. If transcripts are produced from the recording — whether for accessibility, archiving, or AI-assisted grading — the transcript encodes the bias the oral format was supposed to escape. Kaminsky's question about transcripts is load-bearing here: a transcript is *more* legible to the institution than a video (searchable, excerptable, forwardable) and it reads worse than the conversation felt. Halting speech, fillers, self-corrections all translate brutally to text.

Craycraft's 86%/55% gap already tells the PCP team most of what it needs to know: students valued the format; they did not value the default capture. The design question is not "whether to record" but *which of the four purposes* justifies recording in a given course, and what governance comes with each.

### A defensible middle path

Three design moves would let the pilot scale without surrendering either the fairness case for documentation or the authenticity case for conversation.

1. **Unbundle consent.** Separate consents for (a) appeal documentation, (b) calibration use, (c) student self-review, (d) research. Students can opt in to any subset. The syllabus names each purpose, the retention window, and the access list. A student who declines recording gets an undifferentiated alternative (a second examiner with contemporaneous notes; a countersigned rubric) — never framed as a favor or a concession, and producing no worse grade-appeal recourse than consenting.

2. **Default-delete rather than default-keep.** If the justification is appeals, delete at the end of the appeal window. If the justification is calibration, the calibration set is drawn only from students who have given explicit research-use consent. Most of the FERPA exposure collapses the moment retention is bounded.

3. **Transcripts as a secondary, bias-marked artifact — not the record.** If transcripts are produced (for captioning, accessibility, or AI-supported rubric prefill), the authoritative record is the audio/video. Transcripts are marked machine-generated and bias-affected. Faculty do not grade from the transcript alone; TFs do not receive the transcript without the recording. Kaminsky's question — *what are transcripts for?* — gets a concrete answer: captioning for deaf and hard-of-hearing students, search aid for the instructor, input to calibration norming. Not a grading substrate.

The next pilot cohort can test these as variables. Craycraft's 55% will likely move, and we will learn whether the resistance was to *being recorded* or to *not knowing what happens to the recording*. That distinction matters for every downstream rollout.

### The studio environment is its own variable

The pilot's student resistance was to *recording and the studio environment*, not to recording alone. This deserves its own line of work, and the site resources should not fold it into the recording question. A studio — lighting, green screen, microphone, framing — makes oral performance feel like a broadcast, not a conversation. If Kaminsky's "freedom to riff" is the aspiration, the physical setting has to accommodate riffing. A second pilot cohort in a seminar room with a ceiling mic and a laptop camera is a legitimate design variant to test separately from the consent changes above.

## 2. Accommodations — why the written-exam playbook does not transfer

### The central finding, up front

The received assumption is that oral formats are inherently less accessible than written ones. One of the site's own sources — the California community college that shifted finals in-person (EdSurge, 2025) — directly contradicts this: *optional* oral components were found to be *more* accessible for students with test anxiety and learning disabilities than the written alternative. This is important for the PCP framing. The accommodations question is not "how do we make oral assessment accessible"; it is "how do we give students a format choice that actually maps to their needs, when the standard accommodations regime was built for one format only."

The written-exam playbook (extended time, reduced-distraction room, reader, scribe, formula sheet) transfers unevenly to oral assessment. Some elements map directly, others do not map at all, and a few produce new questions that no institutional policy has yet answered.

### Three clusters of students where the playbook breaks down

**(a) Students for whom speech itself is the access barrier.**

- Speech disfluencies, stutters, apraxia, selective mutism. Extended time cannot resolve this. A written or asynchronous alternative may be the only accommodation that measures the learning outcome rather than the disability.
- Deaf and hard-of-hearing students. The National Deaf Center's 2025 survey found 61% of disability services offices now provide auto-captioning, but only 14% of deaf college students rate AI captions as fully accessible in high-stakes contexts. An oral exam for a deaf student likely requires ASL interpretation — which raises the secondary question of whose rendering is assessed (the student's signing, or the interpreter's English). Interpreter scheduling, disciplinary-vocabulary briefing, and record-of-assessment decisions all have to be pre-negotiated.
- Students with anxiety disorders, autism, ADHD. The literature here is genuinely contested. The research Elle compiled on autistic students in higher education and on ADHD students documents significant *format-dependent* performance gaps in oral assessment — not from reduced knowledge, but because oral formats demand simultaneous processing of social cues, verbal formulation, and self-regulation under scrutiny. At the same time, the California community-college finding shows that for some students with test anxiety, oral is easier. "Neurodivergence" is not a single accommodation pattern. The guidance has to be student-led, not diagnosis-led.

**(b) Students for whom the *interactive* nature of the exam creates new inequities.**

- Non-native English speakers. Memon et al. (2010) explicitly warn examiners to focus on professional attributes and not language surface features for second-language candidates, and to receive training in the source and nature of bias. Few institutions actually deliver this training, and the UK gender-bias study (2018) — a gap that widened under male examiners — shows how quickly bias operates when rubrics are unsupported by calibration.
- Students with processing or memory disabilities. "Think on your feet" is an *advertised* feature of oral assessment in the literature. It is also, for some students, exactly the barrier written assessment was compensating for. Open-book and note-accessible oral formats address this partially; formula-sheet analogues (a one-page concept map the student may bring) are worth piloting.

**(c) Students whose accommodation is AI itself.**

Teachers College's existing syllabus language — cited in the National Deaf Center report — allows "generative AI accommodation" for students with a registered disability. An oral exam that prohibits AI raises a question no institutional policy has answered: does a student with an AI accommodation get to use AI during the oral? For preparation only? For real-time transcription of the examiner's questions? This has to be resolved at the institutional level, not per-course.

### What to ask the Harvard Disability Access Office for

The meeting identified the Accessibility office as a contact point. "Standard written-exam accommodations don't straightforwardly transfer" is an accurate diagnosis; the site resources will need to point faculty at something more concrete than "contact DAO." A usable framework — to be drafted jointly with DAO before the second pilot cohort, not after — would specify:

1. **A pre-negotiated default alternative per course or department.** What written, asynchronous-oral, or interpreter-mediated format satisfies the same learning outcome when the oral is itself the barrier. Faculty should not be inventing the alternative at the point of an accommodation request.

2. **A recording-consent accommodation.** Students with accommodations often have stronger privacy concerns: the documented condition is itself a FERPA-protected record, and disclosure via the recording (mannerisms of a tic disorder, visible assistive tech, an interpreter in frame) can out the accommodation to anyone with access to the recording. The consent unbundling in §1 above needs explicit DAO sign-off.

3. **Examiner training on language, disability, and bias.** Memon's recommendation is twenty-five years old and widely endorsed and still largely unmet in practice. The PCP faculty workshop is the natural venue.

4. **Interpreter and captioning logistics.** Scheduling, vocabulary briefing, whose rendering is the record, what gets transcribed, whether CART or ASL is the default.

5. **AI-accommodation scope in oral formats.** What AI use is permitted for students with a registered AI accommodation during an oral — resolved at the institutional level.

Most of this does not yet exist at peer institutions that have already announced oral-exam pilots. Harvard has the advantage, coming later, of being able to build the framework before scaling rather than after.

## 3. Implications for the current deliverables

Three things follow for the resources the team is batching toward the Bok Center site.

- **The "forking tree" guide needs an accommodations branch, not an accommodations footnote.** At each node (class size, course type, stakes, scaffolding), the tree should surface the accessibility question that node changes. A 150-student lecture with recorded AI-augmented oral exams (the Craycraft model) has a different accommodations profile than a 12-person seminar with oral defenses of written work (the Cornell model). The current taxonomy in the meeting summary does not yet cross-reference this.

- **The sample-rubrics deliverable should include an accommodations-accommodating variant for each format.** Not a separate document — a second column in the same rubric, showing what the same criterion looks like when the student is working in a modified modality. This is the cheapest way to make accommodations visible as a design problem rather than a downstream fix.

- **A recording-and-consent template is its own deliverable.** The four-purpose consent unbundling in §1 can be drafted as a one-page template faculty attach to their syllabus, with DAO and General Counsel review before first distribution. This is more useful to faculty than abstract guidance, and it is the kind of concrete artifact the PCP workshop can pressure-test.

## 4. What the pilot has already told us

The most important thing to hold onto, against both the FERPA anxieties and the accommodations uncertainties, is the pilot's own finding: *students valued the oral format*. The 86% number is real. The 55% is not a rejection of oral assessment — it is a specific rejection of the capture apparatus and the studio. This means the design problem is tractable. Recording can be unbundled. Studios can be swapped for seminar rooms. Accommodations can be built in from the start rather than retrofitted. The pilot is, on its own terms, evidence that "the freedom to riff" and "the documentation we need for fairness" can coexist — *if* the documentation is narrow, consented, and governed, and *if* the accommodations framework is in place before scale.

Corbin, Bearman, Boud, and Dawson (2025), in the wicked-problem framing the executive summary cites, offer three permissions: the permission to compromise, the permission to diverge across contexts, and the permission to iterate. The two questions in this report — recording and accommodations — are exactly the places where all three permissions apply. No single answer will serve all courses, all students, or all formats. But the PCP team does not need a single answer. It needs a framework for making the trade-offs visible, locally negotiable, and revisable between cohorts.
