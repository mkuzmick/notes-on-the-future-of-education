# The Forking Tree: A Faculty Decision Guide for Oral Assessment

A working draft from the PCP team, addressing one of the open questions raised in the March faculty meeting: *"What does the forking tree look like?"*

This guide is the navigational companion to two longer reference documents. The [meeting summary](/meeting-summary) is the source taxonomy of ten formats; the [full briefing](/mk-first-draft) is the research base. Two new working papers do the heavy lifting underneath this one: the [sample-rubrics reference](/oral-rubrics) collects published rubrics from across institutional type and time, and the [recording-and-accommodations report](/recording-and-accommodations-report) maps the two hardest practical questions the meeting flagged — what to do about recording, and what extending accommodations beyond the written-exam playbook actually requires. Where this document gestures at a rubric or an equity issue, those companions go deeper. The aim here is to help a faculty member find their pathway in an hour, not to substitute for what they will need from those references once they have.

---

## Why a decision tree, not a single recommendation

The conversation at the March meeting kept circling back to the same thing: **"oral assessment" is not one thing**, and a single template will quietly fail the very faculty we are trying to support. A 12-person seminar's oral final and a 200-student lecture's oral check-in are different pedagogical animals. Pretending otherwise is the surest way to import the failure modes of one into the other — the seminar becomes a dreaded viva, the large-course oral becomes a logistical impossibility and gets cancelled in week ten.

The most useful thing the Bok Center can offer, then, is **a way for faculty to locate themselves**. Four questions usually settle a colleague's pathway within an hour of conversation:

1. **What are you actually trying to assess?** Real-time synthesis? Conceptual fluency? Argumentation under pressure? Professional performance? Metacognition? Each of these wants a different format.
2. **How large is the class, and what teaching team do you have?** This determines whether you can run a high-touch model yourself, need TF infrastructure with norming, or need to think about asynchronous and AI-assisted formats.
3. **What kind of course is it?** Seminar, lecture, lab, studio, methods, capstone, language — each has inherited oral models worth borrowing from rather than inventing.
4. **How much technology and AI tooling do you want involved?** The honest range runs from "none, I want to talk to my students face to face" through "I want help with scheduling and transcripts" to "I want an AI-augmented studio environment with card-deck prompts and machine-assisted grading calibration." All three are defensible. None is obvious.

Two principles from the meeting and the briefing should sit above any pathway choice:

- **Low stakes, high frequency beats high stakes, one shot.** This is the most consistent finding across the post-AI literature. Five-minute weekly check-ins outperform a single hour-long oral final on every dimension we care about — engagement, learning, equity, faculty time, student anxiety. If a colleague is choosing between adding one big oral exam or many small oral moments, the evidence says many small.
- **AI-resistance is a bonus, not the rationale.** Oral formats cultivate synthesis, real-time reasoning, and professional readiness. They were valuable before generative AI and will be valuable after the current panic settles. Framing the conversation as "how do we catch cheaters" attracts the wrong faculty for the wrong reasons; framing it as "how do we test what students actually understand" attracts the right ones.

A third principle, surfaced in the recording-and-accommodations report and worth raising here, sits underneath all the design work that follows: the wicked-problem framing from Corbin, Bearman, Boud, and Dawson (2025) gives faculty *the permission to compromise, the permission to diverge across contexts, and the permission to iterate*. No pathway in this guide is a final answer, and none should be deployed as if it were. The first cohort is the prototype.

## The forking tree

```
START → What are you trying to assess?
│
├── DEPTH OF UNDERSTANDING — "Can the student think with this material in real time?"
│   │
│   └── How big is your class, and how much TF support?
│       ├── ≤ 20, you teach it      → PATHWAY A: Recurring Check-Ins (low lift)
│       ├── 20–60, with TFs         → PATHWAY B: Scaffolded Capstone Oral Exam
│       ├── 60–200, with TF team    → PATHWAY C: Section-Level Oral Defense
│       └── 200+ or non-traditional → PATHWAY E: AI-Augmented Studio Pilot
│
├── INTEGRITY OF AUTHORSHIP — "Did the student actually do the work?"
│   │
│   └── PATHWAY F: Oral Defense of Written Work (the Cornell bolt-on)
│       Works at any size. Lowest curricular lift in this guide.
│
├── PROFESSIONAL OR PERFORMATIVE COMPETENCE — "Can the student do the thing
│   │  the discipline does — diagnose, argue, present, defend, perform?"
│   │
│   └── PATHWAY D: Borrow Your Discipline's Oral Tradition
│       Crit (studio); Board Talk (STEM); Moot (law);
│       OSCE (clinical); Pitch (business); Generals (research seminar).
│
├── METACOGNITION — "Does the student understand how they know what they know?"
│   │
│   └── Skill-Based Presentation (Sarah Craycraft's model — see Pathway E)
│       OR Reflective oral component appended to any of A–F.
│
└── ORACY ITSELF — "Can the student speak with structure, evidence, and presence?"
    │
    └── Build oracy in as one rubric criterion across A–F, OR design a
        dedicated module (see "When oracy is the point" below).
```

This produces six named pathways. A single course can mix them — a seminar might run Pathway A all term and conclude with Pathway B. The pathways are not exclusive categories but reference points to start from. **At every node, an accommodations question changes shape**; the recording-and-accommodations report's central instruction is that the tree needs an accommodations branch, not an accommodations footnote, and each pathway below carries its own brief accessibility note for that reason.

## The ten formats, mapped to the tree

The meeting's taxonomy of ten formats lives inside this tree as follows. The mapping is for orientation; each format can serve more than one purpose.

| Format from the meeting taxonomy | Primary pathway | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| Oral Defense of Written Work | F | Verifying authorship; cheapest entry point |
| Recurring Oral Check-Ins | A | Building habit and depth in small classes |
| The Rubric and the Practice Run | (cross-cutting) | The single highest-leverage intervention; do this whatever else you do |
| The Crit | D | Studio courses; any project-based course |
| Scaffolded Capstone Oral Exam | B | Concentrations, advanced electives, tutorials |
| Performance-Based Assessment | D | Folklore, music, theater, language, design |
| AI-Augmented Oral Exam (Bok pilot) | E | Larger Gen Eds; faculty wanting documentation infrastructure |
| Board Talk | D | Physics, math, engineering, lab sections |
| Generals & Graduate Oral Exams | B | Models for capstone scaffolding; gold standard at high stakes |
| Skill-Based Presentation | metacognition leaf | Any course where reflection on learning is itself a learning outcome |

The Bok pilot's most important design lesson — that the **card-deck prompt system works as a standalone technique even without the full tech setup** — bridges Pathway A and Pathway E. Faculty can borrow the cards without buying the studio.

## Pathway A — Recurring Oral Check-Ins
*The "low stakes, high frequency" pathway. The single best default for small and mid-sized courses.*

**Best for:** seminars, advanced electives, tutorials, methods courses up to about 20 students you teach yourself.
**What it answers:** depth of understanding; engagement; preparation quality.
**Tech footprint:** none required.

### What it looks like

A standing five- to ten-minute oral moment built into every section meeting or every week of office hours. Each student takes a turn explaining one reading, defending one claim from their last paper, or working through one problem aloud. The instructor (or a TF) listens, probes one or two follow-ups, and notes a brief score. The Michigan model showed that adding weekly check-ins like this raised final grades and engagement; the mechanism is the production effect plus the protégé effect — students who know they will speak prepare differently from students who know they will only write.

### Sample rubrics — three published options

The shared design constraint is that the rubric must be **completable in the room**. Three published versions of this principle:

**Pemantle's 0–4 (University of Pennsylvania, advanced calculus).** A student works a problem at the board; the instructor scores in the moment.

| Score | Meaning |
|------:|---------|
| 4 | Nailed it — no substantive help needed. |
| 3 | Solved with some help — hints got them unstuck, but the thinking was theirs. |
| 2 | Struggled but got there — more prompting than should have been needed. |
| 1 | Bits and pieces. |
| 0 | Could not engage. |

Pemantle reports completing each grade during the fifteen-minute slot without post-exam deliberation; under 10% of students request a re-sit, which is granted as another fifteen minutes.

**Akkaraju's mastery / proficiency / not yet (Queensborough Community College, anatomy and physiology).** A three-tier scaffolded mastery rubric applied across weekly five-minute check-ins, with retakes until the target level is reached. At **mastery**, the student explains the concept without prompting, applies it to a novel case, and corrects common misconceptions. At **proficiency**, the student explains with minor prompting and applies to the canonical case from lecture. At **not yet**, the student recalls vocabulary but cannot apply, and rebooks within 72 hours. Akkaraju reports 74% mastery, 90% proficiency, and academic dishonesty "almost irrelevant."

**Michigan's three-level engagement rubric.** For the "argument audit" version, a content-light engagement scale: **engaged** (can restate the central claim, identify where it departs from a prior reading, and name one open question); **present** (can summarize but not situate or extend); **absent from the material** (cannot identify the central claim). The score feeds participation — typically 10–15% of the final grade — rather than replacing written assessments.

A fourth option, the **nursing teach-back rubric** (three minutes: explain the concept to a layperson, identify the most common misconception, connect to a prior concept), is included in the [rubric library](/oral-rubrics) and is especially clean for office-hours deployment.

### Testimonial (in the room at the meeting)

> Lauren Kaminsky's framing of "the freedom to riff" — creating space for genuine intellectual exchange rather than rote performance — is the spirit of this pathway. The rubric exists so you can riff *and* still grade fairly.

### Accommodations notes specific to this pathway

- **Telegraph the schedule** — "you will be the one I ask on Thursday" — so cold-call anxiety converts into preparation time. This single design move is the highest-leverage equity adjustment for check-in formats.
- **Standing alternative for documented accommodations.** A written paragraph submitted before section, in lieu of speaking that week, preserves the practice without forcing a formal accommodations conversation each time.
- **For students with stutters, apraxia, or selective mutism,** asynchronous recorded check-ins (a 90-second voice memo submitted to the LMS) carry the same pedagogical work without the same in-room exposure.

### Getting started

This pathway needs no infrastructure. The minimum viable version is: pick a section meeting next week, tell students at the start that the last fifteen minutes will be five three-minute oral check-ins, and try it. Iterate from there.

## Pathway B — Scaffolded Capstone Oral Exam
*The History & Literature / Social Studies model. The most evidence-rich approach Harvard already has.*

**Best for:** advanced concentrations, junior tutorials, year-long courses culminating in a capstone — typically 20–60 students with a teaching team.
**What it answers:** synthesis across a body of material; ability to defend a position under sustained questioning.
**Tech footprint:** none required; recording optional for grade norming and appeals (see the [recording report](/recording-and-accommodations-report) for governance).

### What it looks like

An hour-long oral exam — the History & Literature pattern: five prepared topics, three examiners, sustained questioning across the topics in any order. The exam is not the whole intervention; the **scaffolding throughout the term is**. The reason this works at Hist & Lit is the three graded oral presentations in sophomore tutorials, the mock oral conducted by two faculty *not* on the real panel, and the explicit naming of learning objectives early. Without the scaffolding, this pathway becomes the UC Davis cautionary tale (twenty-page paper replaced by twenty-minute defense, evaluations down 0.8 points, complaints about anxiety and fairness).

### Sample rubric — the actual History & Literature senior oral rubric

Reconstructed from program materials in the [rubric library](/oral-rubrics). Six criteria, each scored 1–4. Examiners typically recommend High Pass or Distinction for students scoring 3+ across all criteria; a single 1 is usually a failure.

| Criterion | 4 — Mastery | 1 — Emerging |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|
| **Breadth across the list** | Moves between all five topics with facility; draws connections unprompted. | Large gaps; one or more topics inaccessible. |
| **Depth on at least one topic** | Publishable-quality command of at least one topic. | No topic commanded at substantive depth. |
| **Use of evidence** | Cites specific passages, scenes, or data from memory. | Cannot ground claims in text. |
| **Response to questioning** | Takes a hostile question and turns it into a better argument. | Collapses under follow-up. |
| **Methodological awareness** | Names the critical schools or historiographical debates. | Unaware of methodological debates. |
| **Voice** | Thinks aloud, takes risks, self-corrects. | Reading from memory. |

The two-examiner-minimum is non-negotiable here. Inter-rater reliability for unstructured oral exams sits at 0.4–0.6; a second examiner is the cheapest way to push it toward 0.7+. Hist & Lit uses three.

The lower-stakes ramp — the **Hist & Lit sophomore tutorial rubric**, which is shared with students in advance — has six categories: *summary, source analysis, cross-source connections, contextualization, discussion questions, and presentation style*. This is the French *colles* model implemented at Harvard: low-stakes formative practice building toward a high-stakes capstone four years later. **Social Studies'** alternative is a two-stage rubric (canonical reading list + the student's 1,000-word "intellectual autobiography"), running 1.5 hours since 1960. Both are full models worth borrowing wholesale.

### The mock oral is the equity intervention

The **single most effective intervention in the entire literature** is the structured mock. The Stephenson systematic review (2025) reports mocks raising first-time pass rates from 76% to 100% in one study, and 77%+ of students rating them "very or extremely helpful." Hist & Lit's mock is conducted by two faculty members *not* on the real panel and uses a four-dimension diagnostic rubric — navigation, situating the field, productive use of uncertainty, tempo and repair — with no summative grade. **UBC's CTLT** publishes a student-facing reflection instrument used by the student *after* the mock (four prompts: which question caught you off guard, where did you pad or retreat into generality, where did you resist a question you should have engaged, what do you now know you don't know) and reports it nearly doubles retention of feedback.

### Testimonial

History & Literature has run the senior oral **continuously since 1906**. Social Studies has done its 1.5-hour comprehensive oral since 1960, preceded by the intellectual autobiography. These are not pilots. They are evidence that a scaffolded capstone oral, designed deliberately, scales to every senior in a concentration year after year.

### Accommodations notes specific to this pathway

- **Pre-negotiated alternative format**, drafted with the Disability Access Office *before* exam week, not invented at the moment of an accommodations request. This is the top item the recording-and-accommodations report asks DAO to formalize.
- **Examiner training on language and bias** — Memon's twenty-five-year-old recommendation, still largely unmet. The 2018 Studies in Higher Education finding (women systematically lower-scored across ten UK universities, gap widening under male examiners) is mitigated, not eliminated, by structured rubrics. A 60–90 minute calibration session with the examining team is the minimum.
- **Recording governance** matters more here than for low-stakes pathways because the stakes justify retention but also amplify the FERPA exposure. See the four-rationale framework in the [recording report](/recording-and-accommodations-report) before defaulting to "record everything."

### Getting started

If you are not running a concentration with infrastructure, do not start here. Start with Pathway A and *grow into* this format over a year or two. The faculty who run capstone orals well have all done the smaller version first.

## Pathway C — Section-Level Oral Defense
*The mid-sized lecture pathway. Distributes the load across a TF team.*

**Best for:** courses of roughly 60–200 students with three to ten TFs.
**What it answers:** depth of understanding at a scale where you cannot speak to every student yourself.
**Tech footprint:** scheduling tools (Calendly, Zoom booking); optional audio recording for appeals and norming (see [recording report](/recording-and-accommodations-report)).

### What it looks like

Every student sits for a 10–15 minute oral with their TF during reading period or as a section replacement in week 12. TFs draw from a shared question bank — at least three times as many questions as students, so no two consecutive students see the same prompt. Faculty hold a norming session with TFs the week before, scoring two or three sample answers (recorded from a previous year, or improvised) independently and then discussing.

The point of norming is not to eliminate disagreement; it is to **converge the vocabulary** TFs use, so that Student 47 with TF Maria gets the same score as Student 47 would have gotten with TF Daniel. The 2024 *Frontiers in Education* finding — that bias is eliminated when rubrics are used and reappears clearly when they are not — depends on rubrics that have been *used and calibrated*, not merely written.

Catherine Hartmann at the University of Wyoming reported this format took **13 hours total for a 25-student humanities course** — less than the 15+ hours her written midterms and finals had cost. Scaled with TF infrastructure, the time math holds: 150 students × 15 minutes ÷ 6 TFs = roughly four hours per TF, plus norming and grading. Comparable to grading a written final.

### Sample rubric — Princeton McGraw four-criterion

The closest thing to a shared American reference for this pathway is **Princeton's McGraw Center** rubric: four criteria — content knowledge (accuracy, depth, relevance), critical thinking (analysis, synthesis, evaluation), communication (clarity, organization, articulation), and use of evidence (reference to course materials). Level descriptors are written by the instructor; McGraw recommends two pages of exemplar answers per question so TFs can norm-set before the first student arrives.

A condensed weighted version, useful for shorter sessions:

| Criterion | Weight |
|-----------|-------:|
| Content accuracy and conceptual depth | 30% |
| Reasoning and application to a novel case | 30% |
| Use of evidence (course material, examples) | 25% |
| Communication (clarity, structure, handling of "I don't know") | 15% |

Each scored 1–4, totaling 16; convert to course scale.

### Accommodations notes specific to this pathway

- **TF-team variance is the biggest equity risk** in this pathway. The norming session is the equity intervention, not just a logistics step.
- **Recording for appeal protection** is most defensible here precisely because students assigned to TFs they do not know have less recourse than students examined by the instructor of record. Default-delete after the appeal window — see the [recording report](/recording-and-accommodations-report) §1 for the four-rationale unbundling.
- **Scheduling has its own accessibility footprint.** Students with childcare, work, or commuting constraints are systematically disadvantaged by reading-period oral slots; offer at least one weekday-evening and one early-morning window per TF.

### Getting started

The norming session is the prototype. Run one with your TFs in week 8, on three sample answers, before committing to the full deployment. If the norming session goes well, the format will work; if it goes badly, you have caught the problem in advance.

## Pathway D — Borrow Your Discipline's Oral Tradition
*The format already exists. Adapt it; do not invent a new one.*

**Best for:** any course in a discipline with a 50-to-200-year-old oral tradition — clinical, legal, design, performance, business, research seminar.
**What it answers:** professional or performative competence — *can the student do the thing the discipline does?*
**Tech footprint:** whatever the disciplinary tradition uses.

This pathway is a family of formats. The published rubric for each is more developed than for any of the other pathways above, because the disciplines have been refining them for decades or centuries. Excerpts here; full versions in the [rubric library](/oral-rubrics).

#### The Crit (studio disciplines)

The art critique — student presents work, defends choices before peers and instructors. The most-cited published rubric is **Liverpool John Moores University's design-review assessment sheet**: five criteria, each weighted 20%, each scored 1–5.

| Criterion | Top of scale | Bottom of scale |
|-----------|--------------|------------------|
| **Response to brief** | Extends the brief productively; reframes the problem. | Misreads or ignores the brief. |
| **Conceptual development** | Coherent argument traceable through all drawings and models. | No legible concept. |
| **Design resolution** | Plan, section, site, and detail all resolve in relation to the concept. | Fragmentary and unresolved. |
| **Representation** | Drawings and models communicate with precision. | Unreadable. |
| **Oral defense** | Articulates decisions, names precedents, responds to objections with counter-evidence. | Cannot account for choices. |

The crit literature documents what is now canonical: **students repeatedly ask for the rubric *before* the crit, and rarely receive it.** Sharing it is the single most-requested improvement.

**Yale School of Art's MFA group crit** is the open-ended counterpart — no numerical rubric, a written narrative assessment signed by the full faculty against three open criteria (Position / Execution / Reception), demonstrating that a rigorous rubric can be open-ended as long as it is shared and consistent. The **NASAD/NASM accreditation standards** provide the closest thing to an American national reference: technical proficiency, artistic interpretation, professional presentation and response to critique. Three axes, 1–5, used by every accredited art and music school in the country since the 1940s.

#### The Board Talk (STEM)

Standard in physics, math, engineering. Students solve problems at a whiteboard while explaining their reasoning aloud. The board itself is the artifact — photographable, reviewable, harder to fake than a written solution.

The clearest published rubric is **Gardner & Giordano's Bloom-tiered rubric for physics and astronomy** (2025). Three tiers, each scored 0–2, totaling 6:

| Tier | What earns the score |
|------|----------------------|
| **Explain** | Student states the physical principle and derives the governing equation. The question is whether they produce the physics, not only the formula. |
| **Apply** | Student works the problem to a numerical answer, checking units and limits. The best students check limit cases (what happens as *v* → *c*?) without being asked. |
| **Extend** | Student answers a what-if variation — "what if mass is halved?" — demonstrating reasoning about the structure of the problem, not only the instance. |

The **USAFA mathematics department**, after a decade of practice, weights four criteria equally: **set-up** (translates the word problem into a mathematical object correctly), **execution** (mathematical work is correct), **narration** (is the cadet *explaining* the work, or only performing it silently), and **recovery** (when the cadet errs, do they catch and correct, or does it compound). USAFA reports that *recovery* is the single best predictor of downstream success in advanced mathematics — a finding mirrored in Iannone & Simpson's work on oral assessment and deep learning.

#### The Moot (law)

Pioneered at Harvard Law School in 1820. Students argue a hypothetical case before a panel; judges interrupt, redirect, and probe. The format demands oral advocacy under conditions designed to be unpredictable. For undergraduate courses, the lighter version is a structured debate with prepared positions and randomized assignment of side. The **UK Bar Training Course** uses a 60% pass mark set by the Bar Standards Board — proof that oral assessment can have clear, defensible grading standards enforced at the level of professional licensure.

#### The OSCE (clinical sciences)

The Objective Structured Clinical Examination has been the medical-education standard since 1975. Students rotate through stations; each station tests one clinical skill against a predetermined checklist. **OSCEs solved the inter-rater reliability problem 50 years ago** — they raised oral exam reliability from below 0.25 in the 1960s to levels comparable with multiple-choice tests.

The OSCE's most transferable design feature is **the checklist plus the global rating**. A typical fifteen-minute station includes about twenty checklist items ("introduced self, washed hands; screened for red-flag symptoms; summarized the encounter back to the patient before closing"). The global rating runs from **5** (ready to practice independently) down to **1** (not safe). **A student can pass the checklist and fail the global, or vice versa**, and the two scores correct each other. This is the single design feature most worth borrowing for any oral assessment that has a procedural component — the structure transfers cleanly to lab sciences, language proficiency, and any course where students need to demonstrate a sequence of discrete skills plus the integrated judgment to deploy them.

#### The Pitch (business, entrepreneurship)

The case interview structure used by McKinsey, BCG, and Bain — 30-to-60 minute oral problem-solving conversations with structured rubrics and calibrated interviewers. Students must ask clarifying questions, structure problems, work through quantitative analysis verbally, and defend recommendations under questioning. HBS has used the case method since 1908; class contribution accounts for 40–50% of course grades there.

#### The Defense (research seminars, theses)

The viva voce model, scaled down. The UK PhD viva uses a five-category outcome taxonomy (pass with no corrections / minor / major / R&R / fail) standard across Research Council UK institutions. For undergraduates, the relevant adaptation is closer to one hour, two examiners, three prepared topics.

#### Performance-based variants (music, drama, folklore)

Performance disciplines have the most developed rubrics in the corpus. **Music conservatory juries** (NASM-accredited) typically weight: technical accuracy 25%, musicianship 25%, interpretation 25%, stage presence and recovery 15%, oral response 10%. Even in a performance jury, the panel asks why the student took a repeat at measure 47 — performance alone does not constitute the assessment.

**Drama school practical exams** (RADA, Juilliard Drama, Yale School of Drama) use five 1–5 criteria diagnostic of the student's *scene*, not their knowledge about scenes: given circumstances, objective and action, listening and response, specificity of choice, re-direction (when the examiner gives a new instruction, can the actor take the adjustment).

**Folklore & Mythology's** general exam rubric — fidelity, register, embodiment, framing — captures the concentration's philosophical stance: if a discipline studies how human societies preserve knowledge through spoken word, the assessment must involve the spoken word as a genre, not only as a subject.

### A general rubric for any "argue and field questions" version

For courses where students present a position and field unscripted questions but no disciplinary rubric maps cleanly:

| Dimension | What you are listening for | Weight |
|-----------|---------------------------|-------:|
| **Substantive grasp** | Does the student understand the material they advocate for? Can they answer questions outside their prepared remarks? | 30% |
| **Structure of argument** | Is there a clear thesis? Are reasons ordered, supported, connected? | 20% |
| **Use of evidence** | Are course materials, sources, or data deployed accurately and apropos? | 20% |
| **Response to challenge** | When pressed, does the student adjust, concede, or defend skillfully? | 20% |
| **Presence and clarity** | Pace, audibility, handling of difficult moments. | 10% |

The rubric assumes the *content of the discipline* sets the bar for "substantive grasp." A moot court judge expects different command of facts than a thesis defense panel does, and the rubric absorbs that without modification.

### Accommodations notes specific to this pathway

- **The discipline's tradition probably has accommodations precedent.** OSCEs have decades of guidance for deaf, blind, and mobility-impaired candidates; conservatory juries have well-developed protocols for performance anxiety. Borrow the discipline's accommodations playbook along with the rubric.
- **Performance-based formats have the highest ASR-bias exposure.** If the artifact is the performance, recording it is harder to opt out of. The recording-and-consent unbundling matters more here; see the [recording report](/recording-and-accommodations-report).

### Getting started

If you teach in one of these disciplines, **start here.** The work of inventing a format has been done, often a century or more ago. The work that remains is adapting it to your class size and to the competencies you want this term's students to demonstrate. Several Harvard concentrations are willing models — the contact list in the meeting summary appendix is a good place to start.

## Pathway E — AI-Augmented Studio Pilot
*The Bok / Sarah Craycraft model. Highest infrastructure investment; most documentation; emerging best practices.*

**Best for:** larger Gen Eds (75–250 students) where TF-led orals are not feasible at depth; faculty willing to iterate and explicit with students about the tech.
**What it answers:** depth at scale; documentation for grade norming; the integrity question for very large courses.
**Tech footprint:** substantial. Card decks (low-tech version) or full studio environment (high-tech version). **Recording and accommodations governance is more consequential here than in any other pathway**; the [recording report](/recording-and-accommodations-report) is required reading, not optional.

### What it looks like

The Bok Center pilot, run by Sarah Craycraft in a Gen Ed on folklore and performance: students drew cards from thematic decks (Core Concepts, Case Studies, Theoretical Models), prepared for five minutes, then delivered a 4–6 minute spontaneous presentation. The system generated follow-up questions from each student's transcript, and all session data were stored for grading calibration.

The numbers (n=75, >90% response rate): **86% said preparation deepened understanding** — but **only 55% would choose the format again**. The crucial diagnostic from the pilot: **resistance centered on recording and the studio environment, not on the oral assessment itself**. This is good news for the format and a real design constraint for the tech. The recording-and-accommodations report unpacks the four distinct rationales for recording — appeals, calibration, self-review, research — that get conflated in default consent forms, and proposes unbundling them so students can opt in to any subset. That work is upstream of this pathway scaling.

The card-deck prompt system is the bridge: it works as a standalone technique even without the full tech setup. A faculty member who likes the structure (random topic, five-minute prep, brief presentation, examiner follow-up) can run it with index cards on a table and a TF taking notes — no studio required.

### Sample rubrics — three published from AI-augmented pilots

**Craycraft's pilot rubric.** Four criteria, 1–4 each:

| Criterion | 4 — Top | 1 — Bottom |
|-----------|---------|-------------|
| **Concept accuracy** | Explains the drawn concept correctly with nuance. | Cannot define. |
| **Case integration** | Concept does work in the analysis of the case; integration is specific. | Concept and case both present but disconnected. |
| **Theoretical framing** | Places both concept and case within the drawn theoretical model. | Model absent. |
| **Response to AI follow-up** | Uses the follow-up to develop a new line of argument. | Cannot incorporate the follow-up. |

**NYU Stern's voice-agent oral exam** (Ipeirotis, 2025) graded against four machine-scored dimensions: **factual correctness** (does the spoken answer match the reference), **reasoning chain** (steps articulated in the right order), **robustness to follow-up** (does the student hold position under pushback), and **defensive move identification** (when the student says "I don't know," is it an appropriate flag or a dodge). Cost per student was $0.42; grade alignment with human examiners was 89%. The 11% disagreement concentrated on robustness to follow-up — which humans read through tone that transcription flattens. **This is the clearest signal in the literature about which criteria are not yet AI-gradable: the ones that depend on hearing the space around the words.**

**Stanford's Sherpa tool** runs short formative oral check-ins and flags weak responses for instructor review — a four-state flag rather than a rubric: **green** (matches reference understanding), **yellow** (partial match; likely understanding gap), **orange** (confident but incorrect — the Dunning-Kruger signature), **red** (unable to produce a substantive response). Instructors see only the yellow-and-red cohort weekly. This is the best-documented example of AI as the formative layer with human judgment as the summative.

The fourth criterion in the Craycraft rubric — response to AI follow-up — is also the hook for **Skill-Based Presentation** as a sub-format: a course where students choose a skill they must figure out how to learn and present on both the skill and the learning process. This foregrounds metacognition and gives students agency over the content of their oral performance. Craycraft's metacognitive rubric (skill acquisition / method articulation / obstacle diagnosis / transfer) is in the [rubric library](/oral-rubrics).

### Testimonial (Sarah Craycraft, Bok pilot)

> 86% said preparation deepened understanding; only 55% would choose the format again. The asymmetry is the finding.

The pilot is the strongest evidence we currently have at Harvard that **the oral format works** and **the surrounding tech is what students push back against**. That is a much better problem than the reverse.

### Accommodations notes specific to this pathway

This pathway's accommodations footprint is **larger and more contested** than any other in the guide. From the recording-and-accommodations report:

- **AI-as-accommodation has no settled institutional answer.** Teachers College allows generative AI accommodation for students with a registered disability. Whether a student with such an accommodation may use AI during an *oral* exam — for preparation, for real-time transcription, for both — is a question Harvard has not resolved. This needs to be answered at institutional level before scaling, not per-course.
- **ASR transcripts encode bias.** CU Boulder documented 24% lower automatic-speech-recognition accuracy for Black speakers; word error rates of 46–56% for younger speakers whose speech the training corpus underrepresents. If transcripts feed into AI-assisted grading, the transcript carries the bias the oral format was supposed to escape. The defensible position is that **the audio/video is the authoritative record; transcripts are marked machine-generated and bias-affected; faculty do not grade from transcript alone.**
- **Studio environment is a separate variable from recording.** The Craycraft pilot's resistance combined both. A second pilot cohort in a seminar room with a ceiling mic and a laptop camera is a legitimate design variant to test separately.
- **A human appeals path** is mandatory. No grade should rest entirely on a machine-graded transcript without human review available on request.
- **Cards as equity intervention.** Random topic selection reduces the "I prepared the wrong thing" failure mode that disproportionately punishes first-generation students who cannot ask a peer or family member what to expect.

### Getting started

The card-deck version is the prototype. Build three thematic decks of 20 cards each for a single course unit, run it with one section as a low-stakes formative exercise, and learn what the cards need to look like before scaling. The Learning Lab can help with both the cards and (if you want to grow into it) the studio version. **Do not deploy the full studio version without first reading the recording-and-accommodations report and resolving the consent and accommodations questions with DAO and General Counsel.**

## Pathway F — Oral Defense of Written Work
*The Cornell bolt-on. The cheapest entry point in this guide.*

**Best for:** any course at any size where students submit written work and you are concerned about authorship.
**What it answers:** integrity of authorship; whether the student understands what they submitted.
**Tech footprint:** none required.

### What it looks like

Adopt the Cornell English Language Support Office model verbatim, or close to it: students submit a written assignment, then must "be prepared to orally discuss any content included in your paper." The conversation is short — 5 to 10 minutes — and you do not have to do it for every student every time. The mere expectation deters pure AI generation; the occasional spot-check provides verification.

This is the lowest-lift option in this guide because it requires **no curriculum redesign, no scheduling infrastructure, and no new rubric**. A syllabus statement and a willingness to use office hours for verification when something feels off is the entire intervention.

### Sample rubrics — two published options

**The Cornell verdict rubric** (from the [rubric library](/oral-rubrics)). Three states, written for the verification purpose specifically:

| Verdict | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Pass** | Student can explain the central argument, identify and gloss any source they cited, and respond to a single follow-up about a specific claim. |
| **Pass with note** | Student can do the above with prompting; a note in the gradebook flags the exchange for context if a pattern develops. |
| **Concerns** | Student cannot explain the argument or cited sources; conversation triggers a second meeting and a referral conversation about academic integrity. |

This is *not* a numerical rubric because that is not what this format is for. The grade on the paper is the grade. The oral is a verification layer.

**Hartmann's three-dimension scale** (University of Wyoming, 0–3 each, replaces the paper grade rather than augmenting it). For courses where the oral *is* the assessment of the written work:

| Dimension | 3 — Top | 0 — Bottom |
|-----------|---------|-------------|
| **Argument reconstruction** | States their own thesis precisely; identifies the strongest objection. | Cannot state the thesis of their own paper. |
| **Source use** | Explains what each cited source contributes; names alternatives considered. | Sources cannot be described beyond their titles. |
| **Extension under questioning** | Produces new examples, counter-cases, or implications on the spot. | Does not recognize the question. |

Hartmann reports this oral cycle took 13 hours total for 25 students; her written midterm-and-final cycle the prior year had taken 15+ hours. The rubric's brevity is the reason.

### Testimonial

Cornell's policy reads: "Be prepared to orally discuss any content included in your paper." Eight words of policy. That is the whole intervention. The Cambridge MPhil instructor on Reddit in April 2025 captured the spirit even more bluntly: "Let students use AI as much as they want, given the condition that anything AI does wrong is considered their fault." Pathway F operationalizes that condition.

### Accommodations notes specific to this pathway

- **The spot-check must be random or universal.** Targeting specific students for verification reproduces every bias in the briefing's equity section. Either ask everyone (during a single section meeting) or draw names by lottery in advance.
- **Students whose first language is not English** should not be penalized for accent or hesitation in the oral verification. Memon's recommendation that examiners "focus on professional attributes and not language surface features" applies directly here. The Cornell verdict rubric is designed to keep focus on substance, not delivery; a numerical rubric in this pathway is more dangerous than helpful.
- **Documented accommodations** apply normally; a written follow-up answer to a single question can substitute for the oral exchange. This is the only pathway where the substitution is fully clean — the oral is a verification, not an assessment in its own right.
- **Students with speech disabilities** can verify in writing in the original modality; this is *not* an accommodation but a sensible default available to anyone.

### Getting started

Add the Cornell sentence to your syllabus this term. That is step one. Step two is using office hours for one verification conversation in week six, just to discover what the conversation feels like. You can decide whether to scale from there.

## Cross-cutting: the design questions that apply on every path

Four concerns apply to every pathway above and should not be treated as pathway-specific. Two of them — recording and accommodations — are deep enough to warrant their own working paper, and the [recording-and-accommodations report](/recording-and-accommodations-report) is where their full treatment lives. The summaries below are the operational version of what that report concludes.

### The bias finding, and the fix

The 2018 Studies in Higher Education work documenting systematically lower oral-exam scores for women, widening under male examiners, is the most important finding in this section. It does not say "do not use oral exams." It says **do not use oral exams without explicit, predetermined criteria and multiple examiners**. The 2024 *Frontiers in Education* study showed that pre-service teachers' implicit biases against students with migration backgrounds disappeared as a measurable grading effect *when a rubric was used* — and showed up clearly when no rubric was used. The fix is structural, not aspirational.

For every pathway: a written rubric exists before the exam; more than one person is involved in scoring high-stakes versions (B, C, D, E); a norming conversation has happened; recordings (when used) exist for appeals.

### Anxiety, scaffolded

Student anxiety about oral exams is real and documented. The 2021 thematic study identified six drivers: judgment, physical symptoms, uncertainty, comparison with peers, need for practice, and desire for support. The intervention with the largest documented effect is **the practice run**: structured mocks raised first-time pass rates from 76% to 100% in one study, and 77%+ of students found them "very or extremely helpful."

The counter-evidence is also worth holding: a California community college that introduced optional oral components found many students *preferred* them to written tests because timed writing under pressure was actually more stressful for students with test anxiety and learning disabilities. The assumption that orals are universally more anxiety-inducing than writing deserves scrutiny.

### Recording — a designed variable, not a default

Most institutional guides fold all rationales for recording into "record in case of grade appeals." This undersells what recording is for and what governance comes with each purpose. The recording-and-accommodations report identifies **four distinct rationales**, each with a different retention window and access model:

1. **Grade appeals** — until the appeal window closes (30–60 days). Access: student, instructor, dean.
2. **Inter-rater calibration / TF norming** — as long as the norming set is in use. Access: instructor and TFs, training only.
3. **Student self-review** — through the student's degree, at the student's option. Access: the student.
4. **Pilot iteration and research** — separate consent regime under HRPP/IRB. Access: research team only.

**Conflating these is why students resist.** A student who would consent to #1 may refuse #4; the typical consent form does not let them choose. The defensible middle path: **unbundle consent** (separate opt-ins for each rationale), **default-delete rather than default-keep** (FERPA exposure collapses the moment retention is bounded), and treat **transcripts as a secondary, bias-marked artifact — not the record** (faculty do not grade from transcripts alone; TFs do not see the transcript without the recording). The Craycraft pilot's 86%/55% gap suggests resistance was not to *being recorded* but to *not knowing what happens to the recording* — a tractable design problem.

The **studio environment** is a separate variable from recording. A second pilot cohort in a seminar room with a ceiling mic and a laptop camera is a legitimate design variant; do not bundle the two.

Full governance discussion in the [recording-and-accommodations report](/recording-and-accommodations-report) §1.

### Accommodations — beyond the written-exam playbook

The received assumption is that oral formats are inherently less accessible. The California community college finding directly contradicts this: optional oral components were *more* accessible for students with test anxiety and learning disabilities than the written alternative. The accommodations question is not "how do we make oral assessment accessible" but **"how do we give students a format choice that maps to their needs, when the standard accommodations regime was built for one format only."**

The recording-and-accommodations report identifies three clusters where the written-exam playbook breaks down:

**(a) Students for whom speech itself is the access barrier** — disfluencies, stutters, apraxia, selective mutism, deaf and hard-of-hearing students, some students with anxiety/autism/ADHD. Extended time does not resolve speech disfluencies; a written or asynchronous-recorded alternative may be the only accommodation that measures the learning outcome rather than the disability. Deaf students likely require ASL interpretation, which raises the secondary question of whose rendering is assessed.

**(b) Students for whom the *interactive* nature creates new inequities** — non-native English speakers (Memon's recommendation that examiners focus on professional attributes, not language surface features, is widely endorsed and largely unmet), students with processing or memory disabilities (the "think on your feet" feature is, for some, exactly the barrier written assessment was compensating for; open-book and concept-map-permitted oral formats address this partially).

**(c) Students whose accommodation is AI itself** — Teachers College allows AI accommodation for students with registered disabilities; whether AI use is permitted in an *oral* exam under such an accommodation has no settled institutional answer.

What this means at the level of pathway choice: **the tree needs an accommodations branch, not an accommodations footnote**, and each pathway above carries its own brief notes for that reason. What it means at the level of institutional commitment: the meeting's instruction to "contact Harvard's Accessibility Office" is necessary but not sufficient. The recording-and-accommodations report drafts a five-item ask for DAO — pre-negotiated default alternative per course or department; recording-consent accommodation; examiner training on language and bias; interpreter and captioning logistics; AI-accommodation scope in oral formats — that should precede any large-scale rollout.

Full discussion in the [recording-and-accommodations report](/recording-and-accommodations-report) §2.

### When oracy is the point, not a side effect

If the goal of the assessment is *itself* communication — public speaking, interviewing, language fluency, rhetoric — then the pathways above are the wrong frame. A dedicated oracy module weights delivery much more heavily than any rubric here does. The closest published reference is the **AAC&U VALUE rubric for Oral Communication**, used or adapted by over 4,000 institutions: organization, language, delivery, supporting material, central message — each scored 1–4. The VALUE rubric explicitly does *not* assess content knowledge and is therefore insufficient on its own for most oral assessment contexts; it is, however, a useful base layer for the communication-skills portion of any hybrid rubric. The Bok Center is happy to consult on this separately.

## Design principles across the rubric corpus

Reading across the rubrics in the [reference library](/oral-rubrics) — from the 1215 Paris statutes to the 2025 NYU Stern voice-agent protocol — the settled findings are few and durable. They apply to whichever pathway you choose:

- **Share the rubric before the exam.** The most consistent single request in the student-perception literature, from the LJMU crit focus groups to the OSCE guidance to the Stephenson systematic review. Students consistently ask to see the criteria; tutors consistently fail to show them. Fixing this costs nothing.
- **Keep the rubric short enough to complete in the room.** Pemantle's 0–4, Akkaraju's three tiers, the OSCE checklist all sacrifice granularity for completability. Rubrics that require post-hoc reconstruction fail; the grades drift or the notes get lost.
- **Distinguish checklist from global rating.** The OSCE's strongest design feature, since Harden's 1975 paper. A student can pass the checklist and fail the global, or vice versa, and the two scores correct each other.
- **Norm before you grade.** The *Frontiers in Education* finding that rubrics eliminate measurable bias depended on rubrics that had been *used and calibrated*, not merely written.
- **Name the criterion, then define the levels.** "Critical thinking" is a criterion; "analyzes, synthesizes, and evaluates information, identifying at least one alternative framework" is a level descriptor. Criteria without descriptors produce exactly the confusion the crit is notorious for.
- **Handle prompting explicitly.** Pemantle's 3-versus-4 and Akkaraju's mastery-versus-proficiency both answer the question "does prompting cost points?" *in the rubric itself*. Leaving it implicit is a reliability failure waiting to happen.
- **Distinguish formative from summative.** The same instrument should not be used for both — formative encourages openness, summative inhibits it. Interim crit versus final crit is the cleanest expression.
- **Build in a re-sit structure.** Pemantle's fifteen-minute retake; Akkaraju's weekly re-attempt; the UK viva's minor-corrections window. Re-sits reframe the oral from a single performance moment to a mastery check.

## What we still don't know — and what to bring to the next round

The meeting's open questions remain mostly open, and this guide does not resolve them. Naming them honestly is the point of having a working draft:

- **Terminology.** "Oral assessment," "oral assignment," "oral activity," "oral exam" — each carries different connotations. We have used "oral assessment" as the umbrella throughout; faculty buy-in may want different framing for different audiences.
- **Recording and transcripts.** The four-rationale unbundling proposed in the [recording report](/recording-and-accommodations-report) needs General Counsel and Privacy review before it becomes template syllabus language. The question of what transcripts are *for* — Kaminsky's question at the meeting — has a concrete answer in the report (captioning, search aid, calibration input — not a grading substrate) but the institutional framework to enforce that answer does not yet exist.
- **AI as preparation support vs. assessment compromise.** The working principle from the Learning Lab pilots: if the AI tool generates content the student cannot explain back under follow-up, that content is compromise; if the student can, it is preparation. Whether this principle survives translation into policy remains to be seen.
- **Discussion as assessment.** The meeting raised whether group discussion can be graded, the difference between written and oral assignments at a deeper level, and how to establish rules of discussion. This guide does not address those questions; they deserve their own document.
- **The summer workshop.** Format, timing, and scope are still open. The natural use of this guide is as the spine of a workshop in which faculty self-locate on the tree, then break out by pathway with sample rubrics in hand.
- **The accommodations framework with DAO** is the largest piece of unfinished institutional work the meeting surfaced. The five-item ask in the [recording report](/recording-and-accommodations-report) §2 is a starting point, not a finished proposal.

## A note on historical constancy

Reading the [rubric library](/oral-rubrics) in compressed cross-temporal view: in the medieval *disputatio* (Paris, Bologna, c. 1200), the criteria were logical form, mastery of *auctoritates*, response to *objiciens*, and *modus*. At Harvard in 1643, the tutors recorded *memoria*, *ingenium*, and *modus*. The Cambridge Historical Tripos oral around 1800 weighed *Latinitas*, sources, argumentation, and *modus*. History & Literature's senior oral in the 1910s tested breadth, depth, evidence, response to questioning, methodological awareness, and voice. The UK PhD viva in the 2020s assesses original contribution, method defense, and examined ownership of the work.

The **rubric categories — mastery of sources, response under questioning, decorum — have been stable for eight hundred years.** What changes is the corpus of sources, the register of decorum, and the outcome taxonomy. The graduated letter grade is an inheritance of the Cambridge Tripos, not the *disputatio*. The tension between pass/fail authenticity and graduated ranking is older than any of the current debates. Faculty designing in 2026 are not inventing something new; they are inheriting and adjusting a vocabulary that was already worn smooth by the time Harvard opened its doors.
