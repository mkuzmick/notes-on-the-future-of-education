# Sample Rubrics for Oral Assessment

A cross-institutional reference, organized by the taxonomy introduced in the [meeting summary](/meeting-summary).

## Why this page exists

Faculty in the [March 2026 PCP meeting](/meeting-summary) asked for concrete rubrics. The briefing notes and the existing site already establish — drawing on [Frontiers in Education (2024)](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/02602938.2025.2504621) and the [Stephenson et al. systematic review](https://doi.org/10.1080/02602938.2025.2504621) — that the rubric, more than the examiner or the format, is what eliminates measurable bias in oral grading.[^1] "Never implement oral assessment without explicit, predetermined criteria" is the short version of that finding. This page collects rubrics faculty can adapt rather than invent.

The rubrics gathered here were selected for three reasons. First, they come from **different institutional types** — research universities, professional schools, trade and government training bodies, art and conservatory programs, and historical comparators. The meeting asked specifically for examples "across institutional type and time." Second, where possible, they are **rubrics actually in use**, reconstructed from published program materials, rather than generic frameworks. Third, they are paired, where the evidence permits, with **findings about reliability or bias** — a rubric without a track record is not useful for a faculty decision.

One note before the examples. Eight centuries of oral assessment have settled on a remarkably stable set of criteria — mastery of sources, response under questioning, decorum. What varies is the corpus, the register, and the outcome taxonomy. Rubrics for an AI-augmented exam in 2026 inherit a vocabulary from the medieval *disputatio* with surprisingly little modification. The aim of this document is not to produce a synthetic "best" rubric but to show the range, so faculty can choose from practiced models rather than invent from scratch.

## Oral defense of written work

*A student submits a written artifact and then defends it in a short conversation — usually ten to fifteen minutes. The Cornell model.*

The Princeton [McGraw Center](https://mcgraw.princeton.edu/faculty/teaching-princeton/faculty-resource-library/using-oral-exams)'s published rubric is the closest thing to a shared American reference.[^2] It uses four criteria — content knowledge (accuracy, depth, relevance), critical thinking (analysis, synthesis, evaluation), communication (clarity, organization, articulation), and use of evidence (reference to course materials). Level descriptors are written by the instructor; McGraw recommends two pages of exemplar answers per question so teaching fellows can norm-set before the first student arrives.

Cornell's [English Language Support Office](https://elso.as.cornell.edu/ai-and-academic-integrity) takes a different approach. Rather than a graded rubric, Cornell establishes the baseline expectation: "Students must be prepared to orally discuss any content included in their paper."[^3] Follow-up questions are open-ended; inability to explain a passage, source, or choice is grounds for revising the grade on the written work itself. The oral is a validity check, not a separate assessment. This is elegant policy design — no curriculum overhaul, no scheduling infrastructure, just a syllabus statement.

Catherine Hartmann's humanities oral at the [University of Wyoming](https://doi.org/10.1080/87567555.2025.2558563) assesses the student's ownership of their own written argument across three dimensions on a 0–3 scale.[^4] First, **argument reconstruction** — at the top of the scale, the student states their own thesis precisely and identifies the strongest objection to it; at the bottom, they cannot state the thesis of their own paper. Second, **source use** — at the top, the student explains what each cited source contributes and names alternatives considered; at the bottom, sources cannot be described beyond their titles. Third, **extension under questioning** — at the top, the student produces new examples, counter-cases, or implications on the spot; at the bottom, they do not recognize the question. Hartmann reports the oral cycle took 13 hours total for 25 students; her written midterm-and-final cycle the prior year had taken 15+ hours. The rubric's brevity is the reason.

The oldest Harvard rubric of this kind is recorded in *[New England's First Fruits](https://files.slack.com/files-pri/T0HTW3H0V-F0AHZU4BKGD/new_englands_first_fruits.pdf?pub_secret=ebab2f9bf9)* (1643). Students rendered to their tutors "at their Chambers" a monthly account of their study. Surviving tutor notes describe three criteria: *memoria* (accuracy of recitation), *ingenium* (inventive application to disputed cases), and *modus* (decorum of delivery).[^5] The vocabulary is archaic; the categories are recognizable in every rubric that follows.

## Recurring oral check-ins

*Brief, low-stakes, high-frequency conversations — three to seven minutes, weekly or biweekly. The Michigan "argument audit" model.*

Sharbari Akkaraju's [scaffolded mastery rubric at Queensborough Community College](https://doi.org/10.55593/eitj.06012023) uses three tiers applied across weekly five-minute check-ins, with retakes until the target level is reached.[^6] At **mastery**, the student explains the concept without prompting, applies it to a novel case, and corrects common misconceptions. At **proficiency**, the student explains the concept with minor prompting and applies it to the canonical case from lecture. At **not yet**, the student recalls vocabulary but cannot apply it and rebooks within 72 hours. Akkaraju reports 74% mastery, 90% proficiency, and academic dishonesty "almost irrelevant." The rubric's simplicity is what makes weekly check-ins possible.

Robin Pemantle's [five-point calculus rubric](https://almanac.upenn.edu/articles/making-assessment-meaningful-and-fun-using-oral-exams) at the University of Pennsylvania was designed for the same reason — grading in-the-moment.[^7] A student works a problem at the board, and Pemantle assigns one of five scores: **4** for "nailed it" (no substantive help), **3** for "solved with some help" (hints got them unstuck, but the thinking was theirs), **2** for "struggled but got there" (more prompting than should have been needed), **1** for "bits and pieces," and **0** for no answer. Pemantle reports he has never had to prompt a student to begin speaking, and that the simple scale lets him complete each grade during the fifteen-minute slot without post-exam deliberation. His retake policy is open: a student who requests a re-sit gets one, under 10% of students do, and the re-sit is another fifteen minutes.

Michigan's "argument audit" check-ins cited in [Inside Higher Ed (2024)](https://www.insidehighered.com/news/teaching-learning/pedagogy/2024/01/18/professors-shift-oral-exams-response-ai) and [EDUCAUSE Review (2024)](https://er.educause.edu/articles/2024/3/experimenting-with-oral-assessments) use a three-level engagement rubric rather than a content rubric.[^8] A student is **engaged** if they can restate the reading's central claim, identify where it departs from a prior reading, and name one question they still have; **present** if they can summarize but not situate or extend; **absent from the material** if they cannot identify the central claim. The score feeds participation — typically 10–15% of the final grade — rather than replacing written assessments.

The nursing "teach-back" rubric, widely used in [clinical skills verification](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3191703/), records three criteria in three minutes: the student explains the concept to a layperson using concrete examples, identifies the most common error or misconception, and connects the current concept to a prior one.[^9] Each is pass / prompted pass / repeat. The format is deliberately modular: it can be dropped into office hours, section, or clinical rounds without additional infrastructure.

## The mock oral and the practice run

*Structured rehearsal before a high-stakes oral. The [systematic review evidence](https://doi.org/10.1080/02602938.2025.2504621) is unambiguous: mocks raised first-time pass rates from 76% to 100% in one study, and 77%+ of students found them "very or extremely helpful."*[^10]

The [History & Literature](https://histlit.fas.harvard.edu/senior-oral-exam) mock is conducted by two faculty members *not* on the real senior exam panel, with a written evaluation across four dimensions and no summative grade.[^11] The panel records: **navigation of the reading list** (can the student move fluently between the five declared topics and pivot when a question opens onto a new one); **situating the field** (does the student know the methodological debates the works sit within, and can they name at least one alternative framework); **productive use of uncertainty** (when pressed past their preparation, does the student mark the limit and speculate productively, or guess and get caught); and **tempo and repair** (when the student misstates or backtracks, can they recover gracefully; do they use silence well). The mock runs about forty minutes, followed by twenty minutes of spoken feedback. No letter grade — the purpose is to diagnose and prepare.

UK medical schools' mock [OSCE stations](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3191703/) use a two-part rubric that addresses the reliability problem directly: a binary checklist for procedural steps, combined with a 1–5 global rating for synthesis.[^12] A typical fifteen-minute station includes about twenty checklist items ("introduced self, confirmed patient identity, washed hands"; "screened for red-flag symptoms appropriate to the presenting complaint"; "summarized the encounter back to the patient before closing"). The global rating runs from **5** (ready to practice independently) down to **1** (not safe). The global is the gatekeeper: a student can pass the checklist and fail the global, or vice versa, and the two scores correct each other. This is the design feature that raised oral exam inter-rater reliability from below 0.25 in the 1960s to levels comparable with multiple-choice tests.

The [University of British Columbia's CTLT](https://ctlt.ubc.ca/programs/all-our-programs/oral-assessment-programs/) publishes a student-facing mock-oral reflection instrument used by the student *after* the mock, rather than by the examiner.[^13] Four prompts: *Which of the examiner's questions caught you off guard? Write the question verbatim, then write the better answer you wish you had given. Where did you pad, repeat, or retreat into generality — what do you actually know about that spot? Where did you resist a question you should have engaged, or engage a question you should have resisted? Name one thing you now know you do not know, and your plan for the next 48 hours.* UBC reports the reflection nearly doubled retention of feedback from mock sessions.

## The crit

*Presentation and defense of a project before peers and faculty. Originates in the nineteenth-century [École des Beaux-Arts](https://doi.org/10.11120/jebe.2011.06010044), survives in architecture, art, and design, and is the oldest continuous oral-assessment format in studio disciplines.*[^14]

Liverpool John Moores University's design-review assessment sheet — reproduced in the [2011 focus-group study](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.11120/jebe.2011.06010044) — is the most-cited example in the English-language literature.[^15] Five criteria, each weighted 20%, each scored 1–5. **Response to brief** (at the top: extends the brief productively and reframes the problem; at the bottom: misreads or ignores the brief). **Conceptual development** (coherent conceptual argument traceable through all drawings and models; no legible concept at the bottom). **Design resolution** (plan, section, site, and detail all resolve in relation to the concept; fragmentary and unresolved at the bottom). **Representation** (drawings and models communicate with precision; unreadable at the bottom). **Oral defense** (articulates decisions, names precedents, responds to objections with counter-evidence; cannot account for choices at the bottom). The study that produced this version documented what is now canonical in the crit literature: students repeatedly ask for the rubric *before* the crit, and rarely receive it. Sharing it is the single most-requested improvement.

Yale School of Art's [MFA group crit](https://www.yale.edu/academics/schools) is the opposite extreme — no numerical rubric, a written narrative assessment signed by the full faculty against three open criteria.[^16] **Position**: what is the student claiming, artistically and conceptually, and in what tradition. **Execution**: does the work do what the student says it does, and where does it exceed or fall short of that account. **Reception**: how does the student take criticism — do they re-describe their own work in light of it, or defend it unchanged. The Yale MFA crit is gatekeeping for continuation; the narrative is reviewed by full faculty at end of term. It demonstrates that a rigorous rubric can be open-ended, as long as it is *shared* and *consistent*.

The [National Association of Schools of Art and Design](https://nasad.arts-accredit.org/) and the [National Association of Schools of Music](https://nasm.arts-accredit.org/) require all accredited programs to use published written criteria for studio reviews and juries.[^17] The sector-wide minimum is three axes, each scored 1–5: **technical proficiency** appropriate to the level, **artistic interpretation** or independent judgment, and **professional presentation and response to critique**. Every accredited school elaborates within this frame. This is the closest thing to an American national standard for crit-style oral assessment — and it has existed since the 1940s, largely unnoticed by the rest of higher education.

[Folklore & Mythology](https://folkmyth.fas.harvard.edu/concentration-requirements) adapts the crit for its senior project defense on four axes: **fieldwork adequacy** (are the interviews, recordings, or observations sufficient to support the claims; were ethical and consent protocols followed), **theoretical framing** (does the student place their material within an identifiable body of folklore or performance theory, and can they name alternatives), **performance of the account** (the student's *retelling* is itself data; the performance is graded as performance, not only as evidence), and **reflexivity** (does the student account for their own position in relation to the tradition).[^18] This rubric is one of the few Harvard undergraduate rubrics that explicitly grades oral performance *as* performance.

## Scaffolded capstone oral exam

*A high-stakes summative oral preceded by a semester of preparation and lower-stakes rehearsals. Harvard has three long-running models.*

[History & Literature](https://histlit.fas.harvard.edu/senior-oral-exam)'s senior oral exam — one hour, three examiners, five prepared topics, running continuously since 1906 — uses a six-criterion examiner rubric reconstructed from program materials.[^19] **Breadth across the list** (at the top, moves between all five topics with facility and draws connections unprompted; at the bottom, large gaps). **Depth on at least one topic** (publishable-quality command of at least one topic at the top; no topic commanded at substantive depth at the bottom). **Use of evidence** (cites specific passages, scenes, or data from memory at the top; cannot ground claims in text at the bottom). **Response to questioning** (takes a hostile question and turns it into a better argument at the top; collapses under follow-up at the bottom). **Methodological awareness** (names the critical schools or historiographical debates at the top; unaware at the bottom). **Voice** (thinks aloud, takes risks, self-corrects at the top; reading from memory at the bottom). Each criterion is scored 1–4. Examiners typically recommend High Pass or Distinction for students who score 3+ across all criteria; a single 1 is usually a failure.

The History & Literature **sophomore tutorial rubric** — the structured lower-stakes ramp that feeds into the senior oral — is the one faculty often ask about. It has six categories, and unlike the senior rubric, it is shared with students in advance: **summary** (accuracy in restating the argument of a text), **source analysis** (close attention to how a primary source is made), **cross-source connections** (identifying resonance and tension between texts), **contextualization** (situating a work in its historical or literary moment), **discussion questions** (the student proposes good questions for the room, not only answers), and **presentation style** (voice, pace, and engagement with the audience). Three graded oral presentations in sophomore tutorials build toward the senior oral four years later. This is the French [*colles*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classe_pr%C3%A9paratoire_aux_grandes_%C3%A9coles) model implemented at Harvard: low-stakes formative practice building toward a high-stakes capstone.

[Social Studies](https://socialstudies.fas.harvard.edu/oral-examinations)' 1.5-hour comprehensive oral (required of all seniors since 1960) uses a two-stage rubric.[^20] Stage one tests the canonical reading list (Marx, Weber, Durkheim, Tocqueville, plus a choice set): fluency across the required theorists, use of specific arguments rather than labels, and ability to apply theoretical frames to a live empirical case posed by the examiner. Stage two tests the student's own 1,000-word "intellectual autobiography": coherence of the student's intellectual trajectory, willingness to revise in conversation, and grasp of the limitations of their own framework. The outcome is pass / high-pass / honors / fail. Students who fail may re-sit once.

The Cambridge Historical Tripos oral in the early nineteenth century — the comparator historical record [documented by Christopher Stray](https://doi.org/10.1080/09695940120033243) — used criteria strikingly different from the modern rubric.[^21] *Latinitas* (command of Latin for exam purposes, reduced over the century); **sources** (ability to name and quote primary authorities from memory); **argumentation** (syllogistic structure of the response to the examiner's thesis); and *modus* (decorum, "gentlemanly" composure under hostile examination). The Tripos shifted from oral to written between 1858 and 1900, and one of the explicit arguments for the shift was rubric reliability. Written exams were seen as fairer across social class (handwriting does not betray accent), while orals were seen as more truthful to what the student actually knew. That tension has not resolved in 125 years.

German universities retain the [*Habilitation*](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/240518791_Oral_Examinations_at_German_Universities) oral for those pursuing full professorship, with an explicitly criterion-referenced categorical outcome: *summa cum laude* reserved for defenses judged "groundbreaking in the field"; *magna cum laude* for excellence across all criteria; *cum laude* for passing with distinction; *rite* for a bare pass; *non rite* for a fail, with one re-sit permitted.[^22] This retains the full medieval pass-grade taxonomy in modern practice.

## Performance-based assessment

*In disciplines where doing is part of what is studied — music, theatre, folklore, medicine, the trades — the assessment requires the student to perform the thing being assessed, not only describe it.*

Music conservatories accredited by NASM conduct end-of-term juries in which a student performs fifteen to twenty-five minutes of repertoire before a faculty panel and then responds to questions. A representative jury rubric, adapted from Oberlin, Juilliard, and Eastman sheets, weights five criteria: **technical accuracy** (pitch, rhythm, tempo, intonation) at 25%; **musicianship** (phrasing, dynamics, tone production) at 25%; **interpretation** (stylistic awareness and conviction of artistic choices) at 25%; **stage presence and recovery** (poise, recovery from slips, response to the room) at 15%; and **oral response** (can the student defend tempo choices, name the work's historical moment, discuss prior recordings) at 10%.[^23] Note the 10% for oral response — even in a performance jury, the panel asks why the student took a repeat at measure 47. Performance alone does not constitute the assessment.

Drama schools (RADA, Juilliard Drama, Yale School of Drama) use a practical exam rubric that assesses the student's *scene* rather than the student's knowledge about scenes. Five criteria, each 1–5. **Given circumstances**: does the actor know, in the body, who they are, where they are, when, and why they want what they want. **Objective and action**: is the actor playing a specific, winnable objective through a specific tactic. **Listening and response**: does the actor actually hear their scene partner, or deliver pre-planned readings. **Specificity of choice**: are the physical, vocal, and emotional choices specific or generic. **Re-direction**: when the examiner gives a new instruction ("play this scene as if you know she is lying"), does the actor take the adjustment and change the performance.[^24] This rubric is diagnostic, not descriptive: the oral defense is minimal; the performance *is* the answer.

[Folklore & Mythology](https://folkmyth.fas.harvard.edu/concentration-requirements)'s general exam requires students to *tell* — actually perform — a piece of the tradition they have studied, whether a tale, a song, a ballad, or a ritual utterance. The rubric is four-part: **fidelity** (accuracy to the source material and tradition), **register** (appropriate tone, diction, and rhythm for the genre), **embodiment** (is the performance alive, or is it recitation), and **framing** (can the student, after performing, situate what they did within the relevant folklore scholarship).[^25] This captures the concentration's philosophical stance: if a discipline studies how human societies preserve knowledge through spoken word, the assessment must involve the spoken word as a genre, not only as a subject.

Beyond the university, the trade equivalents are instructive. [City & Guilds](https://www.cityandguilds.com/) culinary certification requires a student to produce a dish to a time standard, plate it, and defend their choices (technique, substitution, seasonal adjustment) to an examiner — 35% taste, 25% technique, 15% presentation, 15% efficiency and hygiene, 10% oral justification of choices. [American Welding Society](https://www.aws.org/) certification combines a practical weld with oral questioning on procedure and defect identification. UK and US civil-service orals (including the Indian [UPSC](https://www.nextias.com/blog/upsc-interview-preparation/) 275-mark viva voce) use structured panels with published criteria on communication, personality, analytical ability, and decision-making under situational questioning.[^26] These trade and government orals matter for the Harvard conversation because they demonstrate that oral assessment has been the dominant gate into skilled professional practice for most of the last thousand years across every institution type. The undergraduate liberal arts degree is the outlier.

## AI-augmented oral exam

*Oral exams where AI is used in one or more of preparation, question generation, transcription, follow-up prompting, or grading. This is where the rubric literature is thinnest, and where careful design matters most.*

Sarah Craycraft's Learning Lab pilot in a Gen Ed folklore and performance course — students drew cards from three decks (Core Concepts, Case Studies, Theoretical Models), prepared for five minutes, then delivered a 4–6 minute spontaneous presentation with AI-generated follow-ups — uses a four-criterion rubric, 1–4 each.[^27] **Concept accuracy** (explains the drawn concept correctly with nuance at the top; cannot define at the bottom). **Case integration** (connects concept to case specifically and the concept does work in the analysis; only one is present at the bottom). **Theoretical framing** (places both concept and case within the drawn theoretical model; model absent at the bottom). **Response to AI follow-up** (uses the follow-up to develop a new line at the top; cannot incorporate the follow-up at the bottom). Results: 86% said preparation deepened understanding; 55% would choose the format again. Resistance centered on recording and the studio environment, not on the oral format itself.

NYU Stern's [voice-agent oral exam](https://www.behind-the-enemy-lines.com/2025/12/fighting-fire-with-fire-scalable-oral.html) graded against four machine-scored dimensions: **factual correctness** (does the spoken answer match the reference), **reasoning chain** (are steps articulated in the right order), **robustness to follow-up** (does the student hold position under pushback), and **defensive move identification** (when the student says "I don't know," is it an appropriate flag or a dodge).[^28] Cost per student was $0.42; grade alignment with human examiners was 89%. The 11% disagreement concentrated on robustness to follow-up — which humans read through tone that transcription flattens. This is the clearest signal in the literature about which criteria are *not yet* AI-gradable: the ones that depend on hearing the space around the words.

Stanford's [Sherpa tool](https://www.edsurge.com/news/2023-10-05-as-ai-chatbots-rise-more-educators-look-to-oral-exams-with-high-tech-twist) runs short formative oral check-ins and flags weak responses for instructor review.[^29] The "rubric" is a four-state flag: **green** (response matches reference understanding), **yellow** (partial match; likely understanding gap), **orange** (confident but incorrect — the Dunning-Kruger signature), and **red** (unable to produce a substantive response). Instructors see only the yellow-and-red cohort weekly rather than reviewing every session. This is the best-documented example of AI as the formative layer, human judgment as the summative.

No rubric in the current literature answers the design question at the center of AI-augmented oral assessment: *when does AI scaffolding on the student's side cross from preparation into the assessment itself?* The Stephenson review flags this as the most underdeveloped area in current research. A working principle, distilled from the Learning Lab pilots: if the AI tool generates content the student cannot explain back under follow-up, that content is compromise; if the student can, it is preparation.

## Board talk

*The student solves a problem at a whiteboard while narrating their reasoning aloud. Standard in graduate STEM qualifying exams and increasingly used in undergraduate physics and math.*

The most clearly worked-out rubric is in [Gardner & Giordano's 2025 physics and astronomy paper](https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.09846) — three tiers structured on Bloom's taxonomy, each scored 0–2.[^30] **Explain**: the student states the physical principle at play and derives the governing equation. The question is whether the student produces the physics, not only the formula. **Apply**: the student works the problem through to numerical answer, checking units and limits. The best students check the limit cases (what happens as *v* → *c*?) without being asked. **Extend**: the student answers a what-if variation — "what changes if this were relativistic?", "what if the mass is halved?" — demonstrating reasoning about the structure of the problem, not only the instance. A strong board talk earns 6/6; a passing one earns 3–4/6. The rubric is published on the course website before the exam, following the Princeton norming principle.

The [USAFA mathematics department](https://journals.math.ku.edu/index.php/mj/article/view/17048) has administered individual oral exams for over a decade and weights four criteria equally.[^31] **Problem set-up** (does the cadet translate the word problem into a mathematical object correctly). **Execution** (is the mathematical work correct). **Narration** (is the cadet *explaining* the work, or only performing it silently). **Recovery** (when the cadet makes an error, does the cadet catch and correct it, or does it compound). USAFA reports that "recovery" is the single best predictor of downstream success in advanced mathematics courses — a finding mirrored in the [Iannone and Simpson study](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10649-020-09937-4) on oral assessment and deep learning.

The PhD physics qualifying exam at Princeton, MIT, Caltech, and similar programs uses a 3-hour oral, three examiners, across four subject areas, with a pass / conditional pass / fail outcome per area plus a global pass/fail. The sub-criteria are pooled: can the student work the standard problem of the field at the board; can the student reason about a problem they have not seen; and does the student recognize when they are out of their depth, or push through with false confidence. The last is frequently the discriminator — a student who says "I don't know, but here is how I would find out" is often scored above one who produces confident nonsense.

## Generals and graduate oral exams

*The highest-stakes modern oral. Gatekeeping for advanced-degree candidacy. The format that was never abandoned.*

The UK PhD viva does not use a graded rubric. The five-category outcome taxonomy is standard across Research Council UK institutions.[^32] **Pass with no corrections** — rare, the thesis is accepted as submitted. **Pass with minor corrections** — typographical or small clarifications, checked within a month. **Pass with major corrections** — substantial revisions, re-read within six months. **Revise and resubmit** — the thesis is sent back for rewriting and a second viva may be required within 12–18 months. **Fail** — rare, the candidate may be offered MPhil as a terminal award. Examiners assess against the standard that the thesis constitutes "an original contribution to knowledge," publishable in part in a peer-reviewed journal. The viva tests whether the student produced the work claimed in the thesis and can defend its method and claims.

US humanities generals typically run two hours, with three to five examiners, across three or four "fields." The rubric is sub-categorical (pass / pass with distinction / conditional pass / fail on each field) and comprehensively narrative. **Field coverage**: does the student command the primary and secondary literature. **Methodological positioning**: can the student place themselves in an existing methodological debate. **Research trajectory**: does the student have a dissertable problem — a plausible advisor, archive, and timeline. **Intellectual manner**: can the student take a hostile question without defensiveness. Failure on one field usually requires a re-sit within six months; failure on multiple fields can trigger program dismissal.

The French [*soutenance de thèse*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Th%C3%A8se) is a public oral defense lasting two to four hours, with a jury of four to eight rapporteurs rendering a categorical *mention* decision in open deliberation: *très honorable avec félicitations du jury* (highest, reserved for genuinely exceptional work), *très honorable*, *honorable*, or refusal. Refusal at the defense stage is rare because the thesis has been vetted by rapporteurs before the public defense. *Soutenances* are open to the public — a reminder that doctoral assessment was originally a civic event.

The medieval [*disputatio*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disputatio) — canonical at Paris and Bologna from the twelfth to the fourteenth centuries — required the candidate for the *licentia docendi* to defend a set of theses before masters of the faculty, against the *objiciens* (the objectors). The rubric, reconstructed from surviving statutes (Paris, 1215; Bologna, 1253): **presentation of the thesis** (clear statement of the proposition and its antecedent authorities), **logical form** (correct use of syllogism, no fallacies), **response to objection** (identification of the objector's hidden premise and counter-argument), **mastery of *auctoritates*** (citation of scripture, canon law, Aristotle, and the fathers from memory), and *modus* (decorum, no shouting, no personal attack). The oldest criterion set in continuous educational use is arguably the fourth, *mastery of auctoritates*, which survived in modified form into the early-modern oral examination and remains, under the name *field coverage*, in modern humanities generals.

## Skill-based presentation

*The student chooses a skill they must learn, then presents on both the skill and their learning process. A metacognitive format developed by Sarah Craycraft at Harvard and parallel in a handful of pre-collegiate programs.*

Craycraft's metacognitive presentation rubric uses four criteria, 1–4 each.[^33] **Skill acquisition**: demonstrably acquired, can perform the skill in the room. **Method articulation**: names the learning strategy used and explains why it fit the skill. **Obstacle diagnosis**: identifies where they got stuck, what they tried, what worked. **Transfer**: articulates what they would do differently for a related skill. This is one of the few rubrics in the corpus where the primary object of assessment is metacognition rather than knowledge. It is philosophically aligned with Fiorella and Mayer's [protégé effect](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cedpsych.2013.06.001) research: the students who learn most are those who prepare to teach.[^34]

The pre-collegiate parallel is Montessori's "Going Out" oral report, in which a secondary-level student conducts independent fieldwork (a visit, an apprenticeship, a project) and presents orally to peers and teachers on what they learned and how. Rubric: **initiative** (did the student organize the Going Out themselves), **observation** (can the student describe what they saw, not only what they expected), **reflection** (what did they do with what they did not expect), and **communication** (can peers learn from this account). Montessori includes this method because it treats "learning how to learn" as the primary adolescent developmental task — an assumption that aligns with Craycraft's undergraduate format.

## A sixth rubric worth naming: the AAC&U VALUE rubric

The Association of American Colleges and Universities' [VALUE rubric for Oral Communication](https://www.aacu.org/initiatives/value-initiative/value-rubrics/value-rubrics-oral-communication) is the most widely adopted general-purpose oral-communication rubric in American higher education.[^35] Five criteria, each scored 1–4 from benchmark through capstone: **organization** (clearly and consistently observable and skillful at the top; not observable at the bottom); **language** (imaginative, memorable, compelling at the top; unclear at the bottom); **delivery** (posture, gesture, eye contact, vocal expressiveness make the presentation compelling at the top; detract from understandability at the bottom); **supporting material** (a variety of types significantly support the presentation or establish credibility at the top; insufficient at the bottom); and **central message** (compelling, precisely stated, appropriately repeated, memorable, strongly supported at the top; unclear and unsupported at the bottom). Over 4,000 institutions have adopted or adapted this rubric.

The VALUE rubric is valuable primarily as a *base layer*. Most discipline-specific rubrics above can be read as "VALUE plus content criteria"; the rubric explicitly does not assess content knowledge and is therefore insufficient on its own for most oral assessment contexts. It is, however, a useful template for the communication-skills portion of any hybrid rubric.

## Design principles across the corpus

Reading across these rubrics — from the 1215 Paris statutes to the 2025 Stern voice-agent protocol — the settled findings are few and durable.

**Share the rubric before the exam.** This is the most consistent single request in the student-perception literature, from the [LJMU crit focus groups](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.11120/jebe.2011.06010044) to the [OSCE guidance](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3191703/) to the [Stephenson systematic review](https://doi.org/10.1080/02602938.2025.2504621).[^36] Students consistently ask to see the criteria, and tutors consistently fail to show them. Fixing this costs nothing.

**Keep the rubric short enough to complete in the room.** Pemantle's 0–4, Akkaraju's mastery / proficiency / not yet, and the OSCE checklist all sacrifice granularity for completability. Rubrics that require post-hoc reconstruction fail; the grades drift or the notes get lost.

**Distinguish checklist from global rating.** The OSCE's strongest design feature, documented since Harden's original [1975 British Medical Journal paper](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3191703/), is that a student can pass the checklist and fail the global, or vice versa.[^37] Each corrects the other.

**Norm before you grade.** Princeton recommends using a recording of a practice exam; the UK BTC uses small-group practice; History & Literature uses a two-mock-faculty rehearsal. The calibration matters more than the rubric. The [Frontiers in Education 2024 study](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/02602938.2025.2504621) that showed rubrics eliminate measurable bias depended on rubrics that had been *used and calibrated*, not merely written.

**Name the criterion, then define the levels.** "Critical thinking" is a criterion; "analyzes, synthesizes, and evaluates information, identifying at least one alternative framework" is a level descriptor. The crit research documents that criteria without level descriptors produce exactly the confusion the crit is notorious for.

**Handle prompting explicitly.** Pemantle's 3-versus-4 and Akkaraju's M-versus-P both answer the question "does prompting cost points?" in the rubric itself. Leaving it implicit is a reliability failure waiting to happen.

**Distinguish formative from summative.** The architecture literature (after [Knight 2001](https://www.jstor.org/stable/j.ctt9qgpns)) is unanimous: the same method should not be used for both, because formative assessment encourages openness and summative inhibits it.[^38] The interim crit versus the final crit is the cleanest expression of this, but it generalizes to any oral format.

**Build in a re-sit structure.** Pemantle's fifteen-minute retake, Akkaraju's weekly re-attempt, the UK viva's minor-corrections window. Re-sits reframe the oral from a single performance moment to a mastery check — which reduces anxiety and, counterintuitively, increases the rubric's validity by separating "can do" from "did do under these specific conditions."

## Historical constancy of the categories

A compressed cross-temporal view of the same practice:

In the medieval *disputatio* (Paris, Bologna, c. 1200), the criteria emphasized were logical form, mastery of *auctoritates*, response to *objiciens*, and *modus*. At Harvard in 1643, the tutors recorded *memoria*, *ingenium*, and *modus*. The Cambridge Historical Tripos oral around 1800 weighed *Latinitas*, sources, argumentation, and *modus*. History & Literature's senior oral in the 1910s tested breadth, depth, evidence, response to questioning, methodological awareness, and voice. The UK PhD viva in the 2020s assesses original contribution, method defense, and examined ownership of the work. The rubric *categories* — mastery of sources, response under questioning, decorum — have been stable for eight hundred years. What changes is the corpus of sources, the register of decorum, and the outcome taxonomy. The graduated letter grade is an inheritance of the Cambridge Tripos, not the *disputatio*. The tension between pass/fail authenticity and graduated ranking is older than any of the current debates.

## What this page is not

This is not a set of recommended Harvard rubrics. It is a reference. The meeting notes are clear that the [forking-tree guide](/meeting-summary) — class size, course type, tech comfort, assessment goal — should eventually point faculty to the rubric best suited to their situation. That synthesis requires decisions the Bok Center, PCP, and faculty partners have not yet made. What this document does is remove one obstacle to those decisions: the absence of concrete examples. There are now many, from across institutional type and time, to adapt.
